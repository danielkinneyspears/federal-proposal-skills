# Storyboarding

A storyboard is a one-section planning sheet completed before the section is
drafted. It forces the writer (and the team) to decide what the section is
*for* — its message, its proof, its takeaway — while changing it is still cheap.

## Why storyboard

Drafting without a storyboard means deciding a section's strategy while writing
its prose, which is the most expensive time to decide it. A storyboard separates
the two: decide the strategy on one page, get it reviewed, then draft against an
approved plan. The Pink Team reviews storyboards and early drafts precisely
because fixing a wrong storyboard costs an hour and fixing a wrongly drafted
section costs a week.

## The storyboard sheet

One storyboard per substantive section. It contains:

- **Section** — the outline heading and number.
- **Requirements covered** — the compliance-matrix requirement IDs.
- **Section M factor(s)** — what this section is scored under.
- **Customer issue** — the hot button or concern this section answers, in the
  customer's terms.
- **Theme statement** — the section's governing message (below).
- **Key points** — the three to five points the section makes, in order.
- **Proof points** — the specific evidence behind each key point.
- **Graphic concept** — the main visual and its action caption.
- **Evaluator takeaway** — the one sentence the evaluator should be able to write
  in their notes after reading the section.
- **Page allocation** — from the page budget.
- **Author**.

The evaluator-takeaway line is the discipline. If the team cannot state, in one
sentence, what the evaluator should conclude, the section has no point of view
and the storyboard is not done.

## Writing a theme statement

A section's theme statement is the win theme from `05-win-strategy.md`, expressed
for this section. It states a benefit to the customer, backed by proof, tied to
the customer issue. It is not a label ("Management Approach") and not a feature
("We have a PMO"). It reads like: *Because [discriminator + proof], the customer
gets [benefit], which removes [the customer's concern].*

The theme statement leads the section when drafted — often as the section's
first substantive sentence and echoed in the closing. See
`drafting-proposal-sections` for how it is carried into prose.

## Proof points

Every key point needs proof. The storyboard names it specifically — a past
performance metric, a credential, a named result — so the writer is not inventing
substantiation at the keyboard. A key point with no identified proof point is a
problem to solve at storyboard time: find the proof, or change the point.

## Graphic concepts and action captions

Federal evaluators read graphics first. Each section should plan its main
graphic at storyboard time:

- **Concept** — what the graphic shows and why it advances the section's theme.
  A graphic that merely decorates is wasted page budget.
- **Action caption** — the caption states the *point* of the graphic, not its
  title. Not "Figure 3: Our Management Structure" but "Figure 3: A single
  accountable program manager and co-located leads keep decisions inside 24
  hours, eliminating the coordination delays that slowed the prior contract."
  The evaluator who reads only the captions should still absorb the proposal's
  argument.

## The storyboard review

Before drafting begins, the storyboards are reviewed together — by the capture
lead, the proposal manager, and ideally a Pink Team. The review asks:

- Does every section's theme connect to a real customer issue and a Section M
  factor?
- Is every key point backed by a named proof point?
- Do the storyboards, read in sequence, tell one coherent story, or do the
  themes contradict each other?
- Does any section lack a point of view?

Storyboard problems fixed here cost minutes. The same problems found at the Red
Team cost a recovery cycle.
