# Shaping Response Types

The pre-RFP instruments an offeror responds to, what each is for, and how to
write each well.

## Procurement integrity limits — read first

Pre-RFP engagement is encouraged by the government, but it is governed by strict
rules. Every shaping response must stay inside them:

- **No source-selection-sensitive information.** Never seek, accept, or use
  non-public information about an evaluation, a competitor's proposal, or the
  government's cost estimate. The Procurement Integrity Act prohibits it.
- **Fair and equal access.** Use the public channels (sources-sought notices,
  RFIs, industry days, draft RFPs). Do not seek a private informational
  advantage other offerors are denied.
- **No improper influence.** Shaping means informing and educating the customer.
  It does not mean offering anything of value, pressuring officials, or steering
  an award. Gifts and gratuities to government personnel are tightly restricted.
- **Honesty.** Represent capability, past performance, and status truthfully in
  every response. Sources-sought and RFI responses feed real government
  decisions.
- **Communication blackout.** Once the RFP releases, customer communication is
  restricted to the contracting officer through formal Q&A. Shaping responses
  belong to the pre-RFP period.

When in doubt, the safe path is the public, equal-access channel. This skill
recommends only legitimate shaping and flags — never pursues — anything that
approaches these lines.

## Sources-sought notices

A sources-sought notice is market research: the government asks whether capable
sources exist, often to decide whether to set the acquisition aside for small
business or a socioeconomic category.

How to respond:
- Address exactly what the notice asks — capability, relevant experience, size
  and socioeconomic status, the specific qualifying questions.
- Demonstrate genuine, relevant capability concisely. The government is
  assessing whether qualified sources exist, not reading a proposal.
- **The set-aside angle.** If the responding firm is a small business or in a
  socioeconomic category and would benefit from a set-aside, the response should
  clearly establish that the firm qualifies and is capable — because the volume
  and quality of qualifying responses can drive the set-aside decision. State
  the firm's status accurately; never misrepresent it.
- Be truthful about capability. The government uses these responses to make a
  real acquisition decision.

## Requests for information (RFIs)

An RFI gathers information to shape an eventual acquisition. It is broader than a
sources-sought notice and often asks for input on approach, feasibility,
technology, or market pricing structures.

How to respond:
- Answer the government's questions directly and substantively.
- Where the RFI invites input on approach, offer informed perspective — what is
  technically possible, what good looks like, what risks an approach should
  anticipate. This is legitimate shaping: it educates the customer.
- Demonstrate capability through the quality of the input, not through
  marketing.
- Do not over-disclose the firm's eventual win approach; an RFI response is not
  confidential and competitors may see the shaped result.

## Draft-RFP comments

A draft RFP is released for industry comment and is the single highest-value
shaping vehicle: the government explicitly invites input on scope, Section L,
and Section M before they are final.

How to respond — a comment matrix:

| Section / page | Issue or question | Recommended change | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|

- Comment on what would make the eventual competition **clearer and fairer** —
  ambiguous requirements, an evaluation scheme that cannot distinguish real
  quality, instructions that conflict, unrealistic constraints.
- Frame every comment as improving the solicitation for the government and all
  offerors. A comment that visibly serves only the commenting firm carries no
  weight and signals self-interest.
- The legitimacy test: a recommended change is sound shaping if the government
  would be glad to have made it. A change that helps the firm only by
  disadvantaging competitors is a risk — do not submit it.

## White papers

A white paper is an unsolicited or invited document offered to the customer to
educate them on a problem, a technology, or an approach.

How to write one:
- Lead with the customer's problem and mission, not the firm.
- Teach something genuinely useful — what is possible, what the trade-offs are,
  what good outcomes look like.
- Substance over salesmanship. A white paper that is a brochure educates no one
  and shapes nothing.
- Position the firm's strengths implicitly, by framing the problem in terms
  where the firm is strong — without naming competitors or making unsupported
  claims.

## Capability statements

A capability statement is a concise summary of what the firm does, its
qualifications, past performance, and differentiators, tailored to a customer or
opportunity.

How to write one:
- Tailor it to the specific customer and opportunity; a generic capability
  statement persuades no one.
- Be specific and verifiable — named past performance, real credentials,
  concrete capabilities.
- Keep it concise; it is a door-opener, not a proposal.

## The shaping mindset

Every shaping response works the same way: it makes the customer smarter and the
eventual requirement better, and in doing so positions the responding firm to
compete from strength. Shaping is influence by education. The moment a "shaping"
idea depends on secrecy, misrepresentation, or disadvantaging competitors, it
has stopped being shaping and become a risk.
