# The Evaluator's Perspective

How to score a proposal the way a federal source-selection evaluator does. The
Red Team uses this; it is the difference between a review of opinions and a
review that predicts the government's evaluation.

## Think like the evaluator

A government technical evaluator is assigned a factor, given the proposals and
the Section M criteria, and told to document strengths, weaknesses, and
deficiencies and assign a rating. They are bound to the stated evaluation
criteria — they cannot credit something Section M does not ask for, and they
cannot ignore a requirement Section M does ask for. They work under time
pressure and document their findings in writing. To Red Team a proposal, adopt
exactly that posture: score only against Section M, document everything, and
give no credit the criteria do not authorize.

## The vocabulary of findings

Source selection uses a precise vocabulary. The Red Team uses the same words so
its findings map directly to how the government will score the proposal.

## Strength

A **strength** is an aspect of the proposal that exceeds a requirement in a way
that benefits the government, or that increases the likelihood of successful
performance. Strengths are how a proposal wins a best-value tradeoff. When the
Red Team finds a strength, it confirms the benefit is real, is substantiated,
and is visible to an evaluator — a strength the evaluator will not notice earns
nothing.

## Weakness

A **weakness** is a flaw in the proposal that increases the risk of
unsuccessful performance. It costs evaluation credit and lowers the rating. The
Red Team records what the weakness is, where it is, and how to remove it.

## Significant weakness

A **significant weakness** is a flaw that *appreciably* increases the risk of
unsuccessful performance. It does serious damage to the rating. Significant
weaknesses are recovery priorities, second only to deficiencies.

## Deficiency

A **deficiency** is a material failure to meet a requirement, or a combination
of weaknesses that increases performance risk to an unacceptable level. A
deficiency makes the proposal **unawardable as written**. If discussions are
held, a deficiency must be corrected to remain in the competition; if the award
is made on initial proposals, an uncorrected deficiency loses. Every deficiency
the Red Team finds is the highest-priority recovery item.

## Adjectival ratings

Non-price factors are usually scored on an adjectival scale. Exact scales vary
by agency and solicitation; a common technical/management scale:

- **Outstanding** — proposal meets requirements and indicates an exceptional
  approach; multiple strengths, low risk.
- **Good** — meets requirements, a thorough approach; some strengths, low to
  moderate risk.
- **Acceptable** — meets requirements; adequate approach; risk no worse than
  moderate.
- **Marginal** — does not clearly meet requirements; has not demonstrated an
  adequate approach; significant weaknesses.
- **Unacceptable** — does not meet requirements; has one or more deficiencies;
  unawardable.

The Red Team assigns the rating each factor would draw and lists the strengths
and weaknesses that justify it. Use the solicitation's actual scale if Section M
states one.

## Past performance confidence

Past performance is usually rated as a **performance confidence assessment** —
the government's confidence that the offeror will successfully perform — rather
than on the technical scale. A common scale: Substantial Confidence /
Satisfactory Confidence / Neutral or Unknown Confidence / Limited Confidence /
No Confidence. The Red Team assesses the past performance volume against the
recency, relevancy, and quality standard (see `writing-past-performance`) and
states the confidence rating it would draw.

## Scoring against Section M

1. Take the Section M factors and subfactors in their stated order and
   importance.
2. For each, read the proposal content that addresses it and document every
   strength, weakness, significant weakness, and deficiency — each with a
   location and the evidence.
3. Assign the adjectival rating (or confidence rating) the documented findings
   support.
4. Note where the proposal merely complies but earns no strength on a
   high-importance factor — that is a competitive gap even though it is not a
   weakness.

## The integrated verdict

After scoring every factor, give the integrated read the Source Selection
Authority would make:

- Under **best-value tradeoff** — given these ratings and the assessed
  competitive field, does this proposal's non-price merit justify its price?
  Would it win the tradeoff, or is a competitor offering more value or a better
  price?
- Under **LPTA** — is the proposal cleanly technically acceptable on every
  pass/fail factor, and is the price low enough to win among the acceptable
  offers?

State the verdict honestly: would this proposal, as written, win? If not, the
prioritized findings are the path to making it win — which is the entire point
of running the review before the government does.
