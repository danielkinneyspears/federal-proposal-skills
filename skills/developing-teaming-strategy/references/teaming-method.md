# Teaming Method

How to analyze gaps, choose teammates, structure workshare, and keep teaming
compliant.

## Why teaming is a workstream

Teaming is not a side note to capture — it is a workstream with its own
analysis, decisions, agreements, and deliverables. The team is part of the offer
the government evaluates: its combined past performance, its combined
capability, its small-business participation. A team assembled deliberately is a
strength; a team assembled from whoever was available, late, is a liability that
constrains workshare, dilutes the offer, and creates execution risk.

## The gap analysis

Start from what the prime cannot do alone. Three kinds of gap:

- **Capability gaps** — scope in the requirement the prime does not perform, or
  does not perform well, as core work.
- **Past-performance gaps** — relevant, recent experience the solicitation will
  reward that the prime lacks. Even a capable prime may need a teammate's past
  performance to be competitive on the past-performance factor.
- **Set-aside and socioeconomic gaps** — a status the team must hold (for a
  set-aside the prime cannot meet alone) or a small-business participation level
  the team structure must achieve.

Each gap is a teaming requirement: a specific thing a partner must bring.

## Prime or subcontractor

Before building a team as prime, confirm the prime role is the right one. If the
prime's gaps are large — most of the scope, the dominant past performance, the
qualifying status — the better strategy may be to join another firm's team as a
subcontractor. The teaming strategy states the role decision and its rationale.

## Scoring candidate partners

For each gap, identify candidate partners and score them on:

- **Gap fit** — how completely and credibly the partner fills the gap.
- **Past performance** — the partner's relevant, recent, well-rated experience.
- **Reliability and reputation** — track record as a teammate; will they
  deliver, and deliver the data the proposal needs on time.
- **Cost posture** — whether the partner's rates fit the price-to-win target.
- **Relationship** — prior successful teaming, trust, cultural fit.
- **Exclusivity risk** — whether the partner is likely to team with a
  competitor instead, or on competing bids.

Choose the partner that best fills each gap. Resist adding partners that fill no
identified gap.

## Workshare and structure

Workshare is the division of the contract scope among the prime and teammates.
Sound workshare:

- **Follows capability.** Each teammate performs the scope it is genuinely best
  at. Workshare assigned on relationship alone invites both performance failure
  and evaluator skepticism.
- **Keeps the prime controlling.** The prime must hold a controlling share and
  the integrating role; the government is buying one accountable prime, not a
  loose coalition.
- **Is defensible.** The proposal will have to explain why each teammate has its
  share. The rationale should be capability and past performance.
- **Satisfies subcontracting goals.** Where a small-business subcontracting goal
  applies, the workshare math must meet it.

Note where workshare is contingent on the final RFP scope; the draft RFP may
shift it.

## Small-business participation

Many solicitations require, or reward, small-business participation, and a large
prime may need a subcontracting plan. The teaming strategy should:

- Identify the participation level the solicitation requires or rewards.
- Structure workshare so small-business teammates' shares meet the goal.
- Capture the data the subcontracting plan (where required) will need.

## OCI and compliance screening

Screen the proposed team for:

- **Organizational conflicts of interest** — a teammate whose other contracts,
  access, or relationships could bias performance or give an unfair advantage.
  An unresolved OCI can disqualify the bid.
- **Set-aside compliance** — a teammate or workshare split that breaks the
  team's eligibility for a set-aside (for example, limitations on the share of
  work that may be subcontracted outside the protected category).
- **Conflicting commitments** — a firm teaming simultaneously on a competing
  bid.

This skill flags these issues. Resolving an OCI — mitigation plans, waivers — is
work for contracts and legal counsel, not the skill.

## Agreements and exclusivity

Teaming relationships are made real by agreements:

- **Letter of intent (LOI)** — an early, lighter commitment to pursue together.
- **Teaming agreement (TA)** — the fuller agreement defining workshare,
  exclusivity, and the obligations of each party for the pursuit and the
  resulting subcontract.

Plan which partners need which, what exclusivity to seek (so a teammate does not
also support a competitor), and the timeline to sign — before the relationship
constrains the bid. Drafting and negotiating the agreements themselves involves
contracts and legal counsel.

## Subcontractor data calls

The proposal needs material from each teammate: past-performance write-ups,
key-personnel resumes, labor rates and cost data, certifications and
representations, and OCI disclosures. Each is a data call with an owner and a
deadline tied to the proposal schedule. Late teammate data is a common cause of
proposal crunch — plan the data calls early and track them in the proposal
management action register.
