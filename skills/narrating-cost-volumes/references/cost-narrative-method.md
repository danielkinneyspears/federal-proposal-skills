# Cost Narrative Method

How to write a cost volume narrative that survives the government's cost
evaluation.

## What the cost volume narrative is for

The cost figures answer *how much*. The cost volume narrative answers *why that
much, and why it is credible*. Government cost evaluators assess whether a price
is reasonable, whether (on cost-reimbursement work) it is realistic, and whether
it is consistent with the offeror's technical approach. The narrative is what
lets them answer yes. A cost volume of bare numbers with no explanation invites
every doubt.

## The division of labor

Two different jobs, kept separate:

- **The pricing or finance team** builds the cost model: labor categories,
  hours, direct and indirect rates, fee, escalation, ODCs. They own the numbers.
- **This skill** writes the narrative around those numbers: the basis of
  estimate, the realism and reasonableness narrative, the explanation of the
  approach.

This skill never generates cost figures. If a number is missing, it is requested
from the pricing team. Inventing a rate or an hour count is both wrong and
dangerous — the figure has to reconcile with the offeror's actual cost
accounting system.

## Structuring to the CLINs

The cost volume is organized to Section L and the Section B contract line item
structure. The government evaluates by CLIN and by period of performance
(base and options), so the narrative follows that structure: the evaluator
should be able to take any CLIN and find its costs and their basis without
searching. Total evaluated price usually includes option years; address them.

## The basis of estimate

The basis of estimate (BOE) is the rationale for each significant cost element.
For each, it answers why the number is what it is and ties it to the work:

- **Labor hours and mix** — why this level of effort and this blend of labor
  categories, traced to the tasks in the technical approach and the staffing in
  the management volume.
- **Rates** — the source of the direct and indirect rates (the accounting
  system, forward pricing rates, current actuals) — narrated, not invented.
- **Escalation** — the basis for the year-over-year escalation applied to
  options.
- **Other direct costs** — why this travel, these materials, this equipment,
  tied to the proposed approach.
- **Subcontractor costs** — how subcontractor costs were estimated and reviewed.

A strong BOE traces every element to the work. "Based on engineering judgment"
with no further rationale is the weakest possible BOE and a realism-finding
magnet.

## Reasonableness vs. realism

Two distinct evaluations the narrative supports:

- **Price reasonableness** — whether the price is fair and not too high. Applies
  to all contract types. Supported by competition, comparison to comparable
  prices, and a credible BOE.
- **Cost realism** — whether the proposed cost is realistic for the work the
  offeror proposed. Applies on cost-reimbursement work (FAR 15.404-1(d)). If the
  government finds the cost unrealistically low, it adjusts it upward for
  evaluation, and may lower the offeror's performance confidence. The realism
  narrative shows the proposed cost genuinely matches the proposed approach.

Match the narrative's depth to the contract type. A firm-fixed-price commercial
buy needs far less cost detail than a cost-reimbursement effort with a full
realism analysis.

## The cost-technical consistency check

The single most damaging cost-volume defect is a cost volume that contradicts
the technical and management volumes. Government evaluators routinely cross-check
the two, and an inconsistency reads as either a mistake or a misrepresentation.
Check, item by item:

- **Staffing** — the labor categories and full-time-equivalent levels priced
  match those the technical and management volumes describe.
- **Approach** — the solution priced is the solution proposed. If the technical
  volume promises an extra capability, the cost volume funds it.
- **Period and deliverables** — the period of performance, milestones, and
  deliverables are the same across volumes.
- **Travel and materials** — ODCs priced match the activity the approach
  describes.
- **Subcontractors** — the subcontracted scope priced matches the teaming
  described.

Every inconsistency is flagged for the user and the pricing team. This skill
does not silently reconcile a mismatch by changing a number — it surfaces the
mismatch so the team decides which volume is right.

## What cost evaluators look for

- A price that is realistic for the proposed work — neither padded nor
  implausibly low.
- A clear BOE that traces costs to the technical approach.
- Internal consistency across all volumes.
- Compliance with the Section L cost format and the Section B CLIN structure.
- For cost-reimbursement work, a defensible realism story.
- No price information bleeding into volumes where Section L prohibits it.
