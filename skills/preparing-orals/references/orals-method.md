# Orals Method

How to build and rehearse a scored oral presentation for a federal procurement.

## Orals formats

Federal oral presentations take several forms; the solicitation defines which:

- **Presentation** — the team briefs its approach against the evaluation
  factors, followed by Q&A.
- **Technical demonstration** — the team demonstrates a capability, tool, or
  solution live.
- **Key-personnel interview** — evaluators question proposed key personnel
  directly to assess their qualifications and command of the work.
- **Scenario or sample task** — the team is given a problem and presents or
  works a solution, often with limited preparation time.

Orals may **replace** a written volume (the presentation is the technical
proposal) or **supplement** the written proposal. Know which: it changes how
much the presentation must carry on its own.

## Building the presentation

- **Structure to Section M.** The presentation's segments follow the evaluation
  factors the orals cover. An evaluator scoring factor by factor should find each
  factor clearly addressed.
- **Allocate time by importance.** The most important factor gets the most time.
  Reserve a known block for Q&A and do not let the presentation crowd it out.
- **Lead each segment with a theme.** Every segment carries a win theme — the
  customer benefit first, the discriminator, the proof. The same themes as the
  written proposal, spoken.
- **One idea per slide or segment.** Spoken content moves fast; the audience
  cannot re-read. Make each point land before moving on.
- **Build in handoff points.** Plan where one presenter hands to the next, so
  transitions are clean rather than awkward.

## Slides and visuals

- Respect the slide count and any advance-submission rule. If slides are
  submitted in advance and locked, they cannot be changed on the day — build
  them final.
- Slides support the speaker; they are not the script. Sparse, visual slides
  with a strong spoken narrative beat dense slides read aloud.
- Use graphics with the point stated, as in written proposals. An evaluator
  glancing at a slide should get its message.
- Make slides legible in the room — large type, high contrast.

## Presenter preparation

- **Match presenters to content.** Each presenter takes the segment that fits
  their role and expertise. The proposed program manager usually opens and
  closes and owns the management content.
- **Honor presenter restrictions.** Many solicitations require presenters to be
  proposed key personnel and bar consultants or executives who will not perform
  the contract. Prepare the people who will actually do the work.
- **Prepare the person, not just the slides.** Each presenter knows their
  segment cold, can deliver it within time, and can take questions on it.
- **Plan the logistics** — who runs slides, who tracks time, where each person
  stands, how the team enters and sets up.

## Anticipating evaluator questions

Q&A is where orals are most often won or lost. Build the anticipated-question
list from:

- **Weaknesses in the approach** — anywhere the proposal or presentation is
  thin, the evaluators will probe. Prepare for the questions about the soft
  spots.
- **Risk** — transition risk, staffing risk, technical risk. Expect to be asked
  how each is controlled.
- **Competitive exposures** — the questions that map to where
  `03-competitive-assessment.md` says the team is vulnerable.
- **Anything mentioned but not explained** — a claim made in passing invites
  "tell us more about that."
- **Key-personnel depth** — in interviews, expect detailed questions testing
  whether the named people genuinely command the work.

For each likely question, prepare a concise, honest answer and decide who fields
it. The best Q&A answers are brief, direct, and confident — and truthful.

## Rehearsal

Rehearsal is mandatory, not optional:

- **A full timed run** — confirms the presentation fits the time with margin and
  the handoffs work.
- **A Q&A drill** — a mock evaluator panel asks the hardest anticipated
  questions. This is where weak answers and the wrong responder get fixed.
- **A logistics check** — the room setup, the technology, the materials, the
  entrance.
- **At least two rounds** if time allows — the first exposes problems, the
  second confirms they are fixed.

## Common pitfalls

- Running over time and being cut off before the strongest material.
- Slides so dense the presenter reads them and the evaluators stop listening.
- A confident but wrong or evasive Q&A answer.
- A presenter who does not know their own segment under questioning.
- Treating orals as informal — a relaxed, unrehearsed team against a scored
  rubric loses to a prepared one.
- Ignoring a presenter restriction and fielding someone the rules do not allow.
