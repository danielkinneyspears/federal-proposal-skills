# Federal Proposal Writing

The conventions that make federal proposal narrative score well. Federal
proposal writing is a craft with a narrow purpose: help an evaluator, under time
pressure, find each requirement, see it satisfied, recognize the strength, and
record a favorable rating.

## Know the reader

The evaluator is not the offeror's champion. They are reading many proposals
against a Section M scoresheet, often quickly. They reward proposals that are
easy to score and penalize proposals that make them hunt. Every convention below
serves that reader.

## Theme-first structure

Each section opens with its theme statement — the benefit-led, proof-backed
message from the storyboard — not with a paraphrase of the requirement. The
evaluator should know the section's point of view in the first two sentences.
The theme then recurs: it shapes the section's argument and is echoed in the
close. One section, one theme, threaded.

## Compliance the evaluator can see

Compliance is necessary but not sufficient — and it must be *visible*:

- Use headings that echo Section L and Section M language. If Section M
  evaluates "Transition Approach", the section addressing it is headed
  "Transition Approach".
- Address requirements in the order Section L or Section M presents them.
- Answer the requirement explicitly before elaborating. Do not make the
  evaluator infer that a requirement was met.
- Where a compliance matrix maps a requirement to this section, the section
  visibly answers it.

A proposal that satisfies a requirement but buries the answer can still lose the
point, because the evaluator scored what they could find.

## Features to benefits

A feature is what the offeror has or does. A benefit is what the customer gets.
Federal evaluators score value to the government, so every feature is carried
through to its benefit:

- Feature, stop: "Our PMO uses an earned-value management system."
- Feature to benefit: "Our PMO's earned-value management system gives the
  contracting officer a 30-day forward view of cost and schedule, so problems
  surface while there is still time to act."

The benefit is stated in the customer's terms — cost, schedule, risk, mission —
and, under best-value, this is where price premium is justified.

## Substantiation

Every claim carries proof. Strongest forms: a quantified result from comparable
past performance; an independent rating or certification; a specific, verifiable
fact (named tools, named personnel, exact figures). Weakest: bare assertion and
superlatives ("world-class", "best-in-class", "extensive"). Replace assertion
with evidence. If a claim cannot be substantiated, weaken the claim to what can
be proven, or cut it — never fabricate the proof.

## Ghosting in prose

Where the win strategy identified ghosting opportunities, the prose executes
them as positive, proven statements of the offeror's strength that let the
evaluator infer the contrast. Name no competitor. Make no unsupported negative
claim. The legitimacy test from `developing-win-strategy` applies in the prose.

## Graphics and action captions

Evaluators read graphics and captions first. Every graphic earns its page
budget by advancing the section's argument, and every graphic carries an action
caption that states the *point*, as a full sentence — not a label. An evaluator
who reads only the headings and captions should still absorb the proposal's
case.

## Language and style

- Active voice. The offeror commits in plain terms: "[Offeror] will...".
- Short paragraphs, one idea each. Dense blocks do not get read closely.
- Specific over vague: "within four hours" beats "rapidly".
- Plain words over jargon, except the customer's own terms of art, which signal
  fluency and should be used accurately.
- Consistent terminology — match the solicitation's vocabulary.
- No unsupported superlatives. They read as filler and invite skepticism.

## The persuasion self-check

After drafting, verify:

```
- [ ] The section opens with its theme statement, not a requirement restatement
- [ ] The theme is threaded through the section and echoed at the close
- [ ] Every requirement the section owns is visibly and explicitly answered
- [ ] Headings echo Section L / Section M language
- [ ] Every feature is carried through to a customer benefit
- [ ] Every claim has substantiation; no fabricated proof; superlatives removed
- [ ] The customer (not the offeror) is the subject of the section's argument
- [ ] Every graphic has an action caption stating its point as a sentence
- [ ] The section is within its page budget
- [ ] Under LPTA: the section shows clean acceptability without gold-plating
```
