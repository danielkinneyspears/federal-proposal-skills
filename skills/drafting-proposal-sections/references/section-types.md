# Section Types

Drafting guidance for the section types common to federal technical and
management volumes. Each has a characteristic evaluator concern; drafting to that
concern is what earns the rating.

## Contents
- Technical / solution approach
- Management approach
- Transition / phase-in
- Staffing approach
- Quality control
- Risk management

## Technical / solution approach

*Evaluator concern: will this approach actually work, and is it low-risk?*

- Describe *how* the work will be done, not just *what* will be done. The
  evaluator is scoring the method.
- Show understanding of the requirement before presenting the solution — a brief,
  specific statement of the problem proves the offeror grasps it. Generic
  understanding statements are filler.
- Tie the approach to the customer's environment and constraints, not a generic
  best practice.
- Make the approach concrete: named methods, defined steps, decision points. An
  approach the evaluator cannot picture cannot be scored as low-risk.
- Under best-value, show where the approach exceeds the requirement in a way
  that benefits the customer — that is where strengths are earned. Under LPTA,
  show the approach is sound and acceptable; stop there.

## Management approach

*Evaluator concern: is there a clear, accountable structure that will run this
without the government having to manage it?*

- Show a clear line of authority and a single accountable program manager.
- Describe how decisions get made and how fast — evaluators reward responsiveness.
- Cover communication with the government: reporting, meetings, escalation.
- Address how the offeror manages subcontractors as one accountable team.
- Connect the structure to outcomes: what the customer gets from it (visibility,
  speed, low administrative burden), not an org chart for its own sake.

## Transition / phase-in

*Evaluator concern: will service be uninterrupted, and is transition risk real?*

- Transition risk is a near-universal customer hot button, especially on
  recompetes. Treat this section as a risk-removal argument.
- Give a concrete, time-phased plan: what happens in which week, who does it,
  what the milestones are.
- Address the hardest transition risks directly — staffing continuity,
  clearances, knowledge transfer, system access — rather than asserting a smooth
  transition.
- For an incumbent, the discriminator is continuity and zero transition risk.
  For a challenger, it is a credible, low-risk plan that neutralizes the
  incumbent's continuity advantage.

## Staffing approach

*Evaluator concern: will the right people actually be on the job, on day one and
over time?*

- Show how the offeror will fill the positions — recruiting, retaining, and (for
  recompetes) capturing incumbent staff.
- Address clearances and qualifications honestly and how any gap is closed in
  time.
- Cover surge and turnover: how the staffing model holds up when demand spikes
  or people leave.
- Coordinate with the key-personnel volume (`developing-key-personnel`) — the
  staffing approach is the system; key personnel are the named individuals.

## Quality control

*Evaluator concern: how does the offeror find and fix its own problems before
the government does?*

- Describe a real quality system: what is measured, how, how often, and what
  happens when a metric goes out of tolerance.
- For a PWS, align quality control with the government's QASP — show the
  offeror's self-surveillance meets or anticipates how the government will
  surveil.
- Make it a closed loop: detect, correct, prevent recurrence. A quality section
  that only describes inspection, with no correction loop, reads as weak.

## Risk management

*Evaluator concern: does the offeror see the real risks, and is the mitigation
credible?*

- Identify risks specific to *this* effort. A generic risk register (schedule,
  cost, scope) with generic mitigations earns nothing.
- For each real risk: likelihood, impact, the concrete mitigation, and who owns
  it.
- Naming a genuine risk and showing a credible mitigation builds evaluator
  confidence. Pretending an effort is risk-free reads as naive and lowers it.
- Do not surface a risk so alarming it reads as a reason not to award; choose
  the real, manageable risks and show command of them.
