# Customer Hot Buttons

A win theme that does not connect to a real customer concern is talking to no
one. This file covers how to surface, validate, and prioritize the customer's
hot buttons.

## Contents
- What a hot button is
- Where hot buttons come from
- Types of hot button
- Validating a hot button
- Prioritizing
- From hot button to theme

## What a hot button is

A hot button is an issue the customer cares about intensely enough that it will
shape how they read and score proposals — often below the surface of the stated
requirement. The requirement says what the customer is buying. The hot buttons
say what they are worried about, what they have been burned by, and what they
are really hoping for. Themes are built on hot buttons because that is where the
evaluator's attention already is.

## Where hot buttons come from

- **Customer engagement** — what program-office contacts emphasized, repeated,
  or worried aloud about during capture (captured in `02-capture-plan.md`).
- **Acquisition history** — why the prior contract is being recompeted; problems
  with the incumbent; what changed in the requirement and why.
- **The solicitation itself** — what Section M weights most heavily, what Section
  C dwells on, what the PWS measures. An emphasis in the document is a signal.
- **Mission context** — pressures on the customer organization: budget, oversight,
  schedule mandates, political attention, mission urgency.
- **Q&A and industry-day discussion** — the questions the customer answered at
  length, and the ones they deflected.

## Types of hot button

- **Past pain.** Something went wrong before — a schedule slip, a security
  incident, a transition failure, cost growth. The customer will reward an offer
  that visibly removes the chance of it recurring.
- **Risk fear.** A specific failure the customer dreads, even if it has not
  happened to them. Transition risk, staffing risk, security risk.
- **Mission priority.** A positive outcome the customer is pushing toward — a
  modernization goal, a performance target, a mission capability.
- **Constraint.** A hard limit the customer lives under — a budget ceiling, a
  fixed deadline, a mandate, a workforce limitation.
- **Preference and bias.** A leaning — toward proven low-risk approaches, toward
  innovation, toward continuity, toward a particular methodology.

## Validating a hot button

Treat a hot button as a hypothesis until it has support. For each candidate:

- **Evidence.** What specifically indicates the customer cares about this — a
  statement, a document emphasis, a history? Tag it *known* or *assessed*.
- **Intensity.** Is this a genuine priority or a mild preference? Themes should
  be built on genuine priorities.
- **Reach.** Does it influence an evaluation factor? A hot button the evaluation
  cannot express has limited leverage.

An assumed hot button with no evidence is a guess. Either find evidence (often a
capture action) or mark it low-confidence and do not over-build on it.

## Prioritizing

Not all hot buttons carry equal weight. Rank them by intensity and by how
strongly they connect to the Section M factors. The top few are where win themes
should concentrate. A proposal that addresses the customer's third-tier concerns
brilliantly and misses the first-tier concern loses.

## From hot button to theme

A hot button is only half of a theme. The theme exists where a prioritized hot
button meets a validated discriminator:

- For each top hot button, ask which validated discriminator most directly
  addresses it.
- If a discriminator addresses it — that pairing is a win theme (complete the
  construction in `win-theme-method.md`).
- If *no* discriminator addresses a top hot button — that is a strategy gap.
  The proposal will have to meet that concern on table stakes alone while a
  competitor with a real discriminator there scores against it. Name the gap;
  it is a finding for the capture team and a risk for the bid.
