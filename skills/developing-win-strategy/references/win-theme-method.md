# Win Theme Method

How to test a strength for discriminator status, build a substantiated win

## Discriminator, table stakes, and the test

A **discriminator** is a strength that gives the evaluator a reason to choose
this offer over the others. A **table stake** is a strength the offer needs in
order to be credible, but which competitors have too. Both belong in the
proposal — table stakes for compliance and credibility, discriminators as
themes — but only discriminators win.

Test every candidate strength against four questions. It is a discriminator only
if the answer to all four is yes:

1. **Valuable?** Does this customer actually care about it — does it connect to
   a real hot button or a Section M factor? A strength the customer does not
   value is irrelevant, however impressive.
2. **True?** Can the team genuinely deliver it? Aspirational discriminators
   become performance failures.
3. **Provable?** Is there evidence — past performance, metrics, credentials,
   demonstrable fact? An unprovable claim cannot anchor a theme.
4. **Rare?** Would the competitors in the Black Hat assessment struggle to claim
   the same thing? If every credible competitor can say it, it is table stakes.

A strength that is valuable, true, and provable but *not* rare is table stakes:
state it for compliance, do not build a theme on it. The discipline of this test
is question 4. Capture teams routinely promote table stakes to "discriminators"
because they are real strengths — but if the competitor across the street says
the same sentence, it discriminates nothing.

## Win theme construction

A win theme is not a slogan. It is a structured claim with five parts:

1. **Hot button** — the customer concern or priority the theme serves.
2. **Discriminator** — the validated strength (valuable, true, provable, rare).
3. **Benefit** — what the customer *gets* as a result, in their terms.
4. **Proof** — the evidence that substantiates it.
5. **Factor** — the Section M evaluation factor the theme influences.

Stated as a sentence, a theme reads: *Because [discriminator with proof], the
customer gets [benefit], which addresses [hot button]* — and it is built to be
scored under [factor].

A theme is then *threaded*: it appears in the executive summary, recurs as the
governing message of the relevant proposal section, and is reinforced by the
proof points placed throughout. One theme, stated consistently, repeated where
it counts. A proposal with fifteen "themes" has none.

Most proposals carry three to five win themes — enough to cover the major
evaluation factors, few enough that each is actually repeated and remembered.

## Feature vs. benefit

The most common theme defect is stating a feature and stopping. A feature is
what the team has or does. A benefit is what the customer gets because of it.

- Feature: "Our team holds 40 active TS/SCI clearances."
- Benefit: "The customer's classified work starts on day one — no clearance lag,
  no schedule slip waiting for investigations to close."

The feature is the proof. The benefit is the theme. Always carry the claim
through to the customer's outcome: cost avoided, risk removed, schedule
protected, mission advanced. If a draft theme stops at the feature, it is not
finished.

## Substantiation — proof types

Every theme needs proof. Strongest to weakest:

- **Quantified past performance** — a metric from comparable prior work ("reduced
  processing time 38% on [comparable contract]").
- **Independent validation** — CPARS ratings, awards, certifications,
  accreditations, third-party assessments.
- **Named, verifiable fact** — specific credentials, specific tools, specific
  named personnel and their records.
- **Demonstrable method** — a process or asset the team can show exists.
- **Assertion** — an unsupported claim. Not proof. A theme resting only on
  assertion is cut or downgraded to table stakes until proof is found.

Proof must be specific. "Extensive experience" is an assertion. "Eleven years
operating this exact system for three DoD customers" is proof.

## Mapping themes to Section M

The government scores against the Section M factors and nothing else. Every
theme must map to a factor, or it will not be scored:

1. List the Section M factors and subfactors (or the best pre-RFP read of them).
2. Assign each win theme to the factor it most influences.
3. Check coverage. A factor with no theme is a factor where the proposal will
   merely comply while a competitor's theme scores. A theme mapped to no factor
   is effort the evaluation cannot reward.
4. Weight effort toward the most important factors. Section M's ordering tells
   the team where the award will be decided.

## Legitimate ghosting

Ghosting raises doubt about a competitor's likely approach without naming the
competitor. Legitimate ghosting:

- Targets a *real* weakness in a competitor's likely approach, identified in the
  Black Hat assessment.
- Is framed as a positive claim about the team's strength, with proof, so the
  evaluator draws the contrast themselves.
- Names no competitor and makes no disparaging or unsupported claim about any
  named firm.

Build ghosting language as a normal win theme whose discriminator happens to be
something competitors lack. The theme stands on its own as a true, proven
benefit; the contrast is the evaluator's inference, not the proposal's
accusation.

## Common failure modes

- **Table stakes dressed as discriminators** — failing the rarity test. The
  cardinal error.
- **Feature lists** — themes that never reach the customer benefit.
- **Unprovable themes** — claims with no evidence behind them.
- **Theme inflation** — so many "themes" that none is repeated or remembered.
- **Orphan themes** — themes that map to no Section M factor.
- **Internal voice** — themes about how good the team is, rather than about what
  the customer gets. The customer is the subject of every theme.
