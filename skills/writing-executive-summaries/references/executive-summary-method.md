# Executive Summary Method

What a federal proposal executive summary is, how to structure it, and how to
write it so it does its job.

## What it is and is not

The executive summary is a persuasion document. Its job is to make the evaluator
*want* to award before they reach the detailed volumes, and to frame how they
read everything after.

It is **not** a summary of the proposal. A volume-by-volume recap ("Section 1
describes our technical approach; Section 2 describes our management approach")
wastes the most valuable pages in the document. The evaluator can read the table
of contents themselves.

## Why position matters

Many evaluators read the executive summary first. It sets their expectations and
their initial lean. A strong executive summary makes the evaluator read the
volumes looking for confirmation of a good impression; a weak or generic one
makes them read looking for reasons to doubt. The pages are the same; the
framing they create is not.

## Structure

Structure the executive summary around the **customer and the win themes**, in
this order:

1. **Customer-focused opening** — the customer's mission, the problem, the stakes.
2. **The central message** — in a sentence or two, why this offer is the answer.
3. **Theme blocks** — one short block per win theme (three to five total).
4. **Risk** — a brief, confident treatment of the customer's main risk concern.
5. **Close** — a specific, confident statement of best value and low risk.

Do not structure it by proposal volume. Structure it by what the customer cares
about, in priority order.

## The customer-focused opening

The first paragraph is about the customer, not the offeror. It shows the offeror
understands the customer's mission, the specific problem this acquisition
solves, and what is at stake if it goes wrong. An evaluator reading a
customer-focused opening immediately sees a contractor oriented to *them*.

The opposite — opening with the offeror's founding year, revenue, employee
count, or contract history — signals a vendor-centric proposal and is the most
common executive summary failure. The offeror's credentials belong later, as
proof, not as the opening.

## Theme blocks

Each win theme gets a short block. A theme block:

- Names the customer benefit first — what the customer gets.
- Identifies the discriminator that delivers it.
- Carries proof — a metric, a rating, a named fact.
- Connects to the hot button it serves.

Three to five theme blocks. More than five and none is memorable. Each block is
tight; the executive summary is short by design.

## Addressing risk

The customer's dominant fear — usually transition risk, performance risk, or
staffing risk — is addressed head-on, briefly and confidently. Acknowledging the
real risk and showing it is controlled builds more evaluator confidence than
implying the effort is risk-free. Do not ignore the risk the customer is most
worried about; the silence is louder than the words.

## The close

The close states, specifically and confidently, why this offer is the low-risk
best value for this customer. It refers to the themes and the customer's
outcome. It is not a generic courtesy ("we look forward to the opportunity to
serve you"). The last thing the evaluator reads should be the reason to award.

## Section L pitfalls

- **Page limit.** The executive summary usually has a tight limit. Respect it.
- **Evaluated or not.** Section L or M may state whether the executive summary
  is evaluated. Either way it is read first and matters; but if it is evaluated,
  it is held to the same substantiation standard as the volumes.
- **The "only here" trap.** Some solicitations state that requirement responses
  appearing only in the executive summary will not be credited. When so, every
  substantive claim in the executive summary is also made — and fully supported —
  in the volume that owns the requirement. The executive summary previews the
  case; it does not secretly contain it.

## The self-check

```
- [ ] The opening is about the customer's mission and problem, not the offeror
- [ ] A single central message is clear and everything serves it
- [ ] It is organized by hot buttons and themes, not by proposal volume
- [ ] Each theme block leads with a customer benefit and carries proof
- [ ] The customer's main risk concern is addressed directly and confidently
- [ ] The close is specific about best value, not a generic courtesy
- [ ] It persuades — it would stand on its own to an evaluator who read nothing else
- [ ] It respects the page limit and the Section L "only here" rule
- [ ] No fabricated proof; no unsupported superlatives
```
