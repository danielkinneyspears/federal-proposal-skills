# Pricing Posture by Evaluation Type

The price-to-win posture differs sharply by how the government evaluates price.
Identify the evaluation type first; it governs the whole analysis.

## Contents

## Why this matters first

The same opportunity priced for an LPTA evaluation and for a best-value tradeoff
produces two different target prices and two different bids. Determine the
evaluation type from Section M (or, pre-RFP, from the best available read of the
acquisition strategy) before estimating a target.

## LPTA — Lowest-Price Technically Acceptable

Among proposals rated technically acceptable, the lowest price wins. Exceeding a
requirement earns nothing and only raises price.

PTW posture:
- The target is at or just below the lowest credible competitor price.
- Price exactly to *acceptable* — meet every requirement cleanly, add nothing.
  Gold-plating is pure cost with no scoring benefit.
- The floor is realism. A price so low the offer cannot credibly meet the
  acceptability standard fails the technical gate, and that loses outright.
- The analysis concentrates on being confident about the *lowest* credible
  competitor, because that competitor sets the number to beat.

## Best-value tradeoff

The government may pay a premium for superior non-price merit. Strengths have
cash value.

PTW posture:
- The target is the highest price the team's non-price advantage can defend —
  not the lowest possible price.
- The defensible premium depends on how Section M weights price against
  non-price factors. When non-price factors are "significantly more important
  than price," a real discriminator can justify a meaningful premium. When price
  approaches equal weight, the defensible premium shrinks.
- PTW here works with `developing-win-strategy`: the premium is only defensible
  if the proposal makes the added value visible and ties it to what the customer
  values. A premium with no articulated value is just a high price.
- Pricing far below the field can also hurt — it can read as not understanding
  the work, and it leaves margin on the table that a winning offer did not need
  to give up.

## Cost-reimbursement and cost-realism analysis

On cost-reimbursement contracts the government reimburses allowable cost plus
fee, and it performs a cost-realism analysis (FAR 15.404-1(d)): it assesses
whether each offeror's proposed cost is realistic for the work that offeror
proposed.

PTW posture:
- The proposed cost must be realistic for the proposed solution. If the
  government judges it unrealistically low, it adjusts the cost *upward* for
  evaluation purposes — so an artificially low bid does not even score as low.
- The lever is the *solution*, not the *cost*. To be evaluated at a lower cost,
  propose an approach that genuinely costs less — fewer hours, a leaner labor
  mix the work actually supports — not the same approach with the numbers
  shaved.
- An unrealistically low cost also signals performance risk and can lower the
  non-price confidence rating. It is doubly counterproductive.

## Hybrid evaluations

Many evaluations mix approaches — some factors scored as acceptability gates,
others as tradeoff discriminators; a price factor evaluated for both
reasonableness and, on cost-reimbursement portions, realism. When the evaluation
is hybrid:

- Decompose it. Identify which factors behave LPTA-style (price exactly to
  acceptable) and which behave tradeoff-style (value can justify price).
- Price each portion to its own logic, then reconcile into one coherent bid.
- When the structure is genuinely ambiguous in the solicitation, that ambiguity
  is a question for the contracting officer, not something to guess.
