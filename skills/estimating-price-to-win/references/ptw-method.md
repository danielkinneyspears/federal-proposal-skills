# Price-to-Win Method

How to estimate the customer's budget, estimate competitors' likely prices, and
find the levers that move the team's price.

## Contents

- Estimating a competitor's price
- The price-to-win range and target
- Cost drivers
- Pricing levers

## Estimating the customer budget

The customer's willingness to pay bounds the top of the price-to-win range.
Estimate it from whatever signals exist, and always express it as a range:

- **The prior contract.** If this is a recompete, the prior contract's value is
  the strongest anchor. Adjust for scope changes and for escalation across the
  years since it was awarded.
- **Comparable awards.** Similar work bought by the same or similar customers.
- **A signaled IGCE.** Some solicitations or draft RFPs signal the independent
  government cost estimate or a budget ceiling; treat it as the ceiling.
- **Funding lines.** Public budget documents sometimes identify the program's
  funding.
- **Scope-built estimate.** Where signals are thin, build a rough order of
  magnitude from the scope: staffing level x fully burdened rates x period of
  performance.

State the basis for the estimate. A budget range with a clear rationale is
useful; a confident single number with no basis is not.

## Estimating a competitor's price

For each competitor in `03-competitive-assessment.md`, estimate a price range:

1. **Start from their assessed cost structure.** Large primes generally carry
   higher indirect rates and overhead; small and mid-size firms often run
   leaner. The competitive assessment's profile of each competitor is the input.
2. **Apply their assessed posture.** Aggressive bidders price thin to win;
   market bidders price near the expected band; premium bidders price to value
   and margin. Use the posture assessed in the Black Hat.
3. **Adjust for solution scope.** A competitor proposing a richer or thinner
   solution than the team will price accordingly. Price the *solution they are
   likely to propose*, not the team's solution at their rates.
4. **Adjust for motivation.** A competitor that wants the work badly (an
   incumbent defending a base, a firm entering a strategic market) will price
   more aggressively than one bidding opportunistically.

The result is a range per competitor. The aggressive end of the most aggressive
credible competitor sets the bottom of the price-to-win range.

## The price-to-win range and target

The price-to-win range runs from the lowest credible competitor price (the
floor — going below it wins on price but may not be realistic) to the customer's
budget ceiling (above which the bid is too expensive to be selected).

The **target** sits inside that range, placed according to the evaluation
posture (see `pricing-by-evaluation-type.md`):

- Under **LPTA**, the target is at or just below the lowest credible competitor
  price — but not below a realistic floor.
- Under **best-value tradeoff**, the target is the highest price the team's
  non-price advantage can defend. A genuinely superior offer can win at a
  premium; the size of the defensible premium is a judgment informed by how
  price is weighted in Section M.

## Cost drivers

The elements that most move a federal price, and therefore where PTW analysis
focuses:

- **Labor mix and rates.** The blend of labor categories and seniority, and the
  direct rates for each. The single largest driver on most services work.
- **Level of effort.** The number of full-time equivalents and hours proposed.
- **Indirect rates.** Overhead, general and administrative, and fringe — applied
  on top of direct labor. Structural; hard to move quickly.
- **Fee or profit.** The margin applied; constrained by competition and, on some
  contract types, by regulation.
- **Subcontractor content.** Subcontractor rates, pass-through costs, and the
  make/buy split.
- **Escalation.** The year-over-year rate increase across option years.
- **Materials, travel, and other direct costs.** Smaller on most services work,
  larger on hardware-intensive or geographically dispersed efforts.

## Pricing levers

When the team's cost exceeds the target, these are the levers that can close the
gap. Each has a quantifiable effect and a consequence — name both.

- **Labor mix.** Shift toward more junior or lower-cost categories where the
  work genuinely allows. Consequence: too aggressive a shift weakens the
  technical offer and invites a realism finding.
- **Level of effort.** Reduce proposed hours. Consequence: must stay credible
  against the scope; an unrealistically low level of effort is a realism risk.
- **Subcontractor strategy.** Re-balance the make/buy split toward lower-cost
  performers, or renegotiate subcontractor rates. Consequence: teaming and
  past-performance implications.
- **Indirect rates.** Apply a reduced or project-specific rate where the cost
  accounting system permits. Consequence: must be defensible and consistent with
  the accounting system.
- **Fee.** Reduce the proposed fee. Consequence: direct margin loss; a lever of
  last resort, but real.
- **Escalation.** Lower the escalation assumption on option years. Consequence:
  absorbs future cost growth as risk.
- **Solution scope.** Choose a leaner technical solution that still meets the
  requirement. Consequence: under best-value, can cost non-price strengths.

## Reconciling cost to target

1. Lay the team's bottom-up cost view next to the PTW target.
2. If cost is at or below target, confirm the resulting margin and stop.
3. If cost exceeds target, list the levers, quantify how much each can credibly
   move price, and identify the smallest combination that reaches the target.
4. Check the resulting bid for realism — especially under cost-realism
   evaluation. A target reached only by making the offer unrealistic is not
   reached.
5. If no realistic combination of levers reaches the target, state it plainly.
   That conclusion belongs in the bid/no-bid decision, not buried in a footnote.
