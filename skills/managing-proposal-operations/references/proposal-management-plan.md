# The Proposal Management Plan

What the proposal management plan contains, how to schedule it, and the cadence
that keeps it alive.

## What the plan is for

The proposal management plan is the agreement on how the proposal will be run:
who does what, by when, reviewed how, produced and submitted how. It exists so
that on any day, anyone on the team can see the schedule, their assignments, the
open actions, and the state of every volume. It is a control surface, not a
document to be admired.

## Backward scheduling from the deadline

The proposal due date is immovable. The schedule is built backward from it, so
the plan reveals immediately whether the available time is sufficient.

Working backward from the due date and time, place (typical order):

1. **Submission** — with a deliberate buffer before the actual deadline. Plan to
   submit early; portals fail and uploads run long.
2. **Final production** — assembly, formatting, the final compliance check
   (`checking-submission-compliance`).
3. **Gold Team review** and its recovery.
4. **Red Team review** and its recovery (`reviewing-color-teams`,
   `running-review-recovery`).
5. **Full draft complete.**
6. **Pink Team review** and its recovery.
7. **Storyboards complete and storyboard review.**
8. **Annotated outline complete** (`outlining-proposals`).
9. **Compliance matrix complete** (`shredding-solicitations`).
10. **Kickoff** — today.

Lay these against a calendar that accounts for weekends, holidays, and any
internal approval gates. If the milestones do not fit between kickoff and
submission, the schedule is in crisis *now* — and that is exactly when it can
still be managed (de-scope, add staff, escalate). A schedule that "fits" only by
compressing reviews to nothing is not a schedule.

Reserve recovery time after every review. A review with no recovery window after
it is a review whose findings cannot be worked.

## The volume plan and RACI

The volume plan maps every volume and major section to the people responsible:

- **Volume lead** — accountable for one volume's compliance, quality, and
  schedule.
- **Section author** — writes a section. Exactly one accountable author per
  section.
- **RACI** — for each volume and major section, who is Responsible (does the
  work), Accountable (owns the outcome — one person), Consulted, and Informed.

Cross-check the volume plan against the compliance matrix `Owner` column: every
requirement's owner and every section's author must agree. A section with no
author is the most common cause of a missed requirement.

## The action item register

A single register of every open action: the action, the owner, the due date,
the priority, and the status. It captures everything that is not a scheduled
milestone — a missing piece of source material, an unanswered question, a
graphic to build, a teaming document to finish. The register is reviewed at the
daily cadence and is the team's honest list of what is not done.

## The daily operating cadence

A proposal is run on a daily rhythm:

- A short daily standup: what closed yesterday, what is in work today, what is
  blocked.
- The schedule, action register, and question log updated daily.
- The compliance matrix status reviewed against the schedule — is each volume
  drafting at the pace the schedule requires?
- Blockers escalated the day they appear, not the day they become critical.

The proposal manager owns this cadence. The plan enables it; the cadence is what
actually keeps the proposal on track.

## Production readiness

Production — assembly, formatting, conversion, the final compliance check, and
submission — is planned, not improvised at the end. The plan names who produces
the final package, what the production steps are, how long they take (this is
the buffer in the schedule), and how submission will be performed and confirmed.
Production readiness is verified before the Gold Team, not discovered after it.
