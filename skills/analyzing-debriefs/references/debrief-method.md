# Debrief Method

How to request, read, and learn from a federal procurement debrief.

## What a debrief is

A debrief is the government's explanation, after an award decision, of how an
offeror's proposal was evaluated and the basis for the award. Under FAR Part 15,
unsuccessful and successful offerors may request one. A debrief is meant to be
informative: it explains the evaluation of *your* proposal — its strengths,
weaknesses, deficiencies, and ratings — and the rationale for award. It does not
disclose another offeror's proprietary information, and the depth of comparative
information disclosed is limited.

A debrief is the highest-quality feedback a proposal team will ever get: the
actual customer, explaining the actual evaluation. Treat it as such.

## Debrief rights and timing

- **The request window is short and strict.** After award notification, an
  offeror generally has a brief, defined period to *request* a debrief in
  writing (commonly three days under FAR 15.506). Miss the window and the
  debrief may be offered only at the government's discretion, or not at all.
- **Debrief mechanics vary.** Debriefs may be written, oral, or a combination,
  and the rules differ somewhat for FAR Part 15 procurements and for task orders
  under FAR 16.505.
- **Timing has downstream consequences.** A timely, required debrief can affect
  other procurement timelines. Because the specifics are time-sensitive and
  rule-bound, confirm the current rules for the specific procurement and involve
  contracts counsel where the stakes warrant — this skill does not track the
  precise current deadlines or thresholds.

The practical rule for the skill: when helping request a debrief, treat the
deadline as urgent and surface it first.

## Preparing debrief questions

The government often invites questions. Strong questions are specific and tied
to the evaluation:

- How did the proposal rate on each Section M factor and subfactor, and what
  drove each rating?
- What strengths, weaknesses, significant weaknesses, and deficiencies were
  identified, and where?
- How was past performance assessed?
- To the extent the government will disclose: how did the proposal's evaluated
  cost or price compare, and what was the rationale for the best-value tradeoff
  or LPTA decision?
- Were there compliance or format issues?

Tie questions to the team's open uncertainties — the places where the win
strategy made a bet, where the Red Team disagreed with the team, where the
outcome was a surprise. Avoid arguing with the evaluation during the debrief; the
purpose is to learn, not to relitigate.

## Reading a debrief

- **Capture verbatim first.** Record what the government said in its own words,
  organized by evaluation factor, before interpreting. Interpretation layered on
  top of an accurate record is useful; interpretation that replaces the record
  is self-deception.
- **Weaknesses and deficiencies are the lesson.** They tell the team exactly
  where the proposal fell short of the standard.
- **Read the win debrief too.** A win debrief shows what worked — and often
  reveals weaknesses the proposal carried despite winning. Those are free
  lessons for the next, possibly closer, competition.
- **Watch for the discriminator that did not land.** When the government did not
  credit something the win strategy treated as a discriminator, that is a
  high-value finding: either it was not truly a discriminator, or the proposal
  failed to make it visible and provable.

## The protest boundary

A debrief occasionally surfaces something that looks like an irregularity — an
evaluation that appears not to follow the stated criteria, or a misreading of the
proposal. This is where the skill stops and counsel begins:

- The skill **does not assess whether a protest has merit** and **does not
  advise whether or how to protest.** That is a legal judgment.
- The skill **flags** a possible irregularity plainly, recommends it go to
  contracts counsel, and notes that protest timelines are short and
  rule-bound — so a decision, if any, must be made quickly by counsel.
- Most debrief findings are lessons, not protest grounds. Disagreeing with the
  government's judgment is not, by itself, a basis for protest. Keep the default
  framing as learning, not litigation.

## Win/loss lessons learned

Convert the debrief into reusable lessons. A good lesson:

- States what specifically happened (from the debrief, not from feeling).
- States what the team will do differently.
- Names where the change applies — bid/no-bid criteria, capture activities,
  proposal practices, pricing posture, review rigor.

Be honest. A loss blamed entirely on an unfair government, a wired incumbent, or
bad luck produces no lesson and repeats the loss. Find the factors the team
controlled. Likewise, do not let a win hide its weak spots.

## Feeding the lessons forward

The pipeline is a loop. Debrief lessons feed the next cycle:

- Lessons about which opportunities are winnable feed `qualifying-opportunities`
  — the bid/no-bid criteria and pWin calibration.
- Lessons about customer or competitor reads feed `planning-capture` and
  `analyzing-competitors`.
- Lessons about proposal quality feed the drafting and review skills.

A debrief analysis that is filed and never consulted has wasted the best
feedback in the business. Its value is realized only when the next pursuit is
run differently because of it.
