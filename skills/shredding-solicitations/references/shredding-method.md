# Shredding Method

How to extract requirements from a federal solicitation and cross-walk them.

## The goal

A shred produces a complete, traceable inventory of everything the offeror must
do, address, or win on. Complete: nothing is missed. Traceable: every item cites
its source. Atomic: every item is one separately verifiable obligation.

## Requirement language

Federal solicitations signal obligation through specific words. Treat each as a
requirement to capture:

- **"shall", "must", "will" (directed at the offeror or contractor)** — a hard
  obligation. Always a matrix row.
- **Instructional imperatives in Section L** — "Provide...", "Describe...",
  "Submit...", "The Technical Volume shall address...". Each is a requirement.
- **Section M evaluation language** — "The Government will evaluate...". Each
  evaluated element is a requirement to win on, not merely address.
- **"should", "may", "is encouraged"** — not mandatory, but capture them: they
  signal what the customer wants and often map to discriminator opportunities.
  Mark them as non-mandatory in the matrix.

Do not soften or harden the language. A "shall" is a shall; a "should" is a
should. Record what the document says.

## Atomic requirements

A single sentence often contains several requirements. "The Technical Volume
shall describe the staffing approach, the transition plan, and the quality
control process, and shall not exceed 30 pages" is four requirements: describe
staffing, describe transition, describe quality control, and observe a 30-page
limit. Split every compound into atomic rows. Compound rows hide obligations and
make verification unreliable.

## Extracting from Section L

Section L tells the offeror how to prepare and submit. Extract:

- The volume structure and what each volume contains.
- The required order and section headings within each volume.
- What each section must address, in detail.
- Page limits, format rules, and submission mechanics (these also go to the
  format register).
- Anything the instructions prohibit.

Section L is usually the organizing axis of the matrix, because the proposal is
physically built to it.

## Extracting from Section M

Section M tells how the government evaluates. Extract:

- Every factor and subfactor, and their relative importance or weighting.
- Every element the government says it will evaluate within each factor.
- The evaluation approach (tradeoff vs. LPTA; see the solicitation primer).
- The basis of award and any minimum acceptability standards.

Each Section M element is a row, flagged as evaluated — the proposal must score
well on it, not merely mention it.

## Extracting from Section C / SOW / PWS / SOO

This is the requirement — the work itself. Extract:

- Every task, performance requirement, and standard.
- Every deliverable and its due date (cross-check the CDRLs).
- For a **PWS**: the required outcomes and the standards/metrics by which
  performance is measured. The QASP indicates how the government will surveil.
- For a **SOO**: the government's objectives. The offeror proposes the SOW/PWS;
  every objective must be traceable to something in the proposed approach, so
  each objective is a requirement row.

## The L/M/C cross-walk

The cross-walk links the three views of the same procurement so the proposal
team can see, for any requirement, where it is written (L), how it is scored
(M), and what work it represents (C). For each requirement, populate:

- its source section and paragraph;
- the Section L instruction that governs where/how it is written;
- the Section M factor or subfactor that evaluates it;
- the Section C/SOW/PWS requirement it implements.

Not every requirement touches all three. A pure format rule lives only in L. A
cross-walk's value is making the partial ones visible.

## Conflicts, ambiguities, and gaps

The cross-walk surfaces three problems the team must act on:

- **L/M conflict** — Section L tells the offeror to address something one way,
  Section M evaluates it another way. Flag it; usually a CO question.
- **Gap** — a Section M factor with no corresponding Section L instruction (how
  is the offeror to know where to write it?), or a Section C requirement no
  factor evaluates. Flag it.
- **Ambiguity** — a requirement that can be read more than one way. Flag it as a
  candidate CO question.

This skill flags these; it does not resolve them by guessing. The contracting
officer resolves them through the formal question process.

## Amendments and Q&A

Every amendment can change requirements, Section L, Section M, or the due date.
After incorporating an amendment, re-shred the affected sections — do not assume
a change is small. Government Q&A responses clarify intent and sometimes change
requirements; treat each substantive answer as a potential matrix update and
note its source.
