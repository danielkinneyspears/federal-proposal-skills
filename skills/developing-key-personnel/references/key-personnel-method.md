# Key Personnel Method

How to extract position requirements, map a candidate to them, and write a
resume that scores.

## Why key personnel are evaluated separately

Key personnel are the specific individuals the government is counting on, named
and contractually committed. The government evaluates them because the people
are a large part of what it is buying — and because, on services contracts,
performance follows the staff. The evaluator's job is to confirm each named
person meets the stated qualifications and, where the evaluation is graded, to
judge how strong they are. The resume's job is to make that easy.

## Extracting position requirements

For each key position, the solicitation states qualifications across Section L,
the position descriptions in Section C or H, and the evaluation criteria in
Section M. Extract them as a discrete checklist:

- **Education** — degree level and field; whether equivalent experience may
  substitute.
- **Experience** — years, type, and recency; experience in specific domains,
  technologies, or environments; management or leadership experience.
- **Certifications and licenses** — named, current.
- **Clearance** — level required and whether it must be active at proposal,
  award, or start.
- **Other** — citizenship, location, special qualifications.

Mark each criterion mandatory (a minimum) or desired (rewarded but not required).

## Matching and checking minimums

Assign a candidate to each position and check them against every mandatory
minimum. Three outcomes:

- **Meets all minimums** — proceed to the mapping.
- **Meets minimums via a stated substitution** — for example, additional
  experience substituting for a degree, where the solicitation allows it. State
  the substitution explicitly.
- **Misses a mandatory minimum** — a compliance failure. Flag it now. Under a
  pass/fail key-personnel evaluation, one missed minimum can render the proposal
  unawardable. The options are a different candidate or, rarely, a CO question
  about the requirement — never hiding the gap.

## The qualification mapping

For each candidate, map experience to required qualifications, criterion by
criterion:

| Required qualification | Mandatory? | This candidate's matching evidence |
|---|---|---|
| BS in a technical field | Yes | BS, Computer Science, 2008 |
| 10+ years relevant experience | Yes | 14 years in federal network operations |
| PMP certification | Yes | PMP, active since 2015 |
| Active Secret clearance | Yes | Active Secret, current |
| Experience with the customer's environment | Desired | 6 years supporting the same DoD component |

The mapping is the analytical backbone. The resume then presents the same match
in narrative form.

## Tailoring a resume

A tailored resume is built for this position on this solicitation:

- **Lead with the match.** Open with a summary that hits the position's required
  qualifications directly, so the evaluator sees compliance immediately.
- **Order by relevance.** Emphasize the experience most relevant to this work
  and this customer. Recent, relevant roles get the most space.
- **Quantify.** Replace duty descriptions with accomplishments and numbers —
  scale managed, results delivered, performance improved.
- **Cut the irrelevant.** Experience that does not serve the match is noise that
  pushes the evaluator past the page limit. Remove it.
- **Show the beyond-minimum strengths.** Where Section M grades personnel, make
  the qualifications that exceed the minimum visible — that is where a resume
  earns a strength rather than a pass.

## Resume format

Section L usually prescribes resume format, content, and length. Follow it
exactly — required headings, the order of elements, the page or word limit.
Where Section L is silent, a conventional structure works: summary of
qualifications, education, certifications, clearance, then relevant experience
most-recent-first. Consistency across all key personnel resumes signals a
disciplined offeror.

## Commitment and availability

A named key person the offeror cannot actually field is a problem waiting to
surface. For each person, confirm the availability evidence:

- **Current employees** — confirm they are assigned and available.
- **Contingent hires** — a signed contingent-hire agreement or letter of
  commitment, stating the person will join if the contract is awarded.
- **Teaming partner personnel** — confirmed through the teaming agreement.

Where Section L requires letters of commitment, include them. Propose only
people realistically available; a "bait and switch" — naming strong personnel
with no intent or ability to deliver them — is an integrity problem and a
performance failure.

## Substitution

Federal contracts typically restrict substituting key personnel after award,
often requiring the substitute to meet or exceed the original's qualifications
and the contracting officer to approve. This is not part of the proposal resume,
but it is why naming committed, available people matters: the offeror is making
a binding commitment, not a suggestion.
