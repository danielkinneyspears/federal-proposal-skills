# Solutioning Method

How to design a proposal-ready solution: the workshop, the elements, and the
three standards every design is checked against.

## What solutioning produces

Solutioning produces a designed solution: a coherent, concrete answer to *how
the work will be done* that is compliant, differentiated, and affordable. Its
output is not prose — it is decisions. The proposal's narrative skills then
present those decisions persuasively. When solutioning is skipped, each author
invents their piece of the solution independently, and the volumes describe a
team that does not agree with itself.

## The solutioning workshop

Solutioning is best done as a working session (or series) with the people who
understand the work, the customer, and the cost — not as a solo drafting task.
Whether it is a half-day task-order session or a multi-week workshop series, the
shape is the same:

1. Frame the design problem: the requirements, the win themes, the cost target,
   the customer hot buttons, the constraints.
2. Design each solution element against that frame.
3. Check the integrated design against the three standards.
4. Resolve trade-offs and record the decisions.

The solution design artifact is the workshop's record.

## The solution elements

A federal services solution is usually designed across these elements. Each is
designed concretely — named methods, defined structure, specific decisions —
concrete enough that a section author can describe it without inventing.

- **Technical approach** — how the work itself gets done: the methods, tools,
  processes, and the decisions that make the approach sound and low-risk for
  this customer's environment.
- **Staffing model** — the labor mix, the organizational structure, how the
  positions are filled and retained, how surge and turnover are handled. The
  staffing model must be consistent with what the cost volume will price.
- **Management approach** — the management structure, lines of authority, the
  single accountable program manager, decision-making, and government
  communication.
- **Transition approach** — the time-phased plan to stand up (or take over) the
  work without service interruption. Usually a major customer hot button.
- **Risk approach** — the real, effort-specific risks and the credible
  mitigations.
- **Quality approach** — how the team measures and controls its own performance,
  aligned to the government's surveillance (the QASP, for a PWS).

The elements must cohere: the staffing model supports the technical approach,
the management approach runs the staffing model, the transition approach stands
all of it up. A design where the elements contradict each other becomes a
proposal an evaluator can pick apart.

## Designing against the three standards

Every design decision is made against three standards at once:

- **Requirements** — the design must satisfy every requirement in the compliance
  matrix. Map elements of the design to the requirements they satisfy; a
  requirement no element addresses is a hole.
- **Win themes** — the design must actually deliver the discriminators the win
  strategy claims. If the win strategy says the discriminator is a proven
  transition method, the transition approach must *be* that method. A
  discriminator the design does not deliver is a theme the proposal cannot
  honestly make — fix the design or fix the strategy.
- **Cost** — the design must be deliverable within the price-to-win target (see
  design-to-cost below).

A solution designed against requirements alone is compliant and forgettable.
Designed against themes alone, it is differentiated and non-compliant. Designed
against cost alone, it is cheap and loses on merit. The design must satisfy all
three.

## Trade-offs

Solutioning is a series of trade-offs:

- **Richness vs. cost** — a more capable approach usually costs more.
- **Innovation vs. risk** — a novel approach can differentiate, or can read as
  risky.
- **Scope of the offered approach** — doing more than the minimum can earn
  strengths or can gold-plate.

Resolve each trade-off consciously, governed by the evaluation approach. Under
**best-value tradeoff**, added value can justify added cost — lean toward the
design that earns strengths on the important Section M factors. Under **LPTA**,
there is no credit for exceeding the requirement — lean toward the cleanest,
leanest design that is solidly acceptable. Record each trade-off decision and
its rationale in the solution design, so the proposal is consistent and the team
does not reopen settled questions mid-draft.

## Design-to-cost

Design-to-cost means the price-to-win target is an input to the design, not a
number checked afterward. The design and the cost are developed together:

- Know the price-to-win target before designing (`04-price-to-win.md`).
- As each element is designed, keep a running sense of what it costs to deliver.
- When the design's cost exceeds the target, redesign — a leaner labor mix the
  work genuinely supports, a different make/buy split, a more efficient method —
  rather than discovering the gap when the cost volume is built.
- If no compliant, theme-delivering design fits the target, that is a real
  finding: surface it to pricing (`estimating-price-to-win`) and capture
  (`qualifying-opportunities` at the bid-validation gate). A solution that
  cannot be priced to win is not a solution, and pretending otherwise just
  moves the loss later.
