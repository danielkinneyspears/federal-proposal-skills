# Customer Engagement and Requirement Shaping

How to build a customer call plan and shape a requirement legitimately — and the
ethical and legal limits that govern it. Read the limits first.

## Contents

- What legitimate shaping looks like

- Building the call plan

## The limits — read first

Customer engagement during capture is normal, expected, and encouraged by the
government — agencies *want* industry input before they finalize a requirement.
But it is governed by strict rules. This skill must stay inside them and must
never recommend crossing them.

- **No source-selection-sensitive information.** Never seek, accept, or use
  non-public information about an evaluation, a competitor's proposal, the
  government's evaluation criteria before they are public, or the independent
  government cost estimate. The Procurement Integrity Act prohibits this. If a
  government contact offers such information, the correct action is to decline
  it and report it — not to use it.
- **Fair and equal access.** Once an acquisition is underway, agencies must give
  competitors equal access to information. Do not seek a private informational
  advantage that other bidders are denied. Public Q&A, industry days, and draft
  RFPs are the proper channels.
- **No improper influence.** Engagement means informing and educating the
  customer about what is possible. It does not mean offering anything of value,
  pressuring officials, or steering an award. Gifts and gratuities to government
  personnel are tightly restricted.
- **Communication blackout.** After the RFP releases, customer communication is
  typically restricted to the contracting officer and the formal Q&A process.
  The call plan's customer-contact actions belong to the pre-RFP period; after
  release, the plan tracks only formal channels.
- **Honesty.** Represent the team's capabilities and past performance truthfully
  in every engagement. Misrepresentation is both an integrity violation and a
  losing strategy.

When in doubt, the safe path is the public, equal-access channel. This skill
recommends only legitimate engagement and should explicitly flag — not pursue —
anything that approaches these lines.

## What legitimate shaping looks like

Requirement shaping is helping the customer write a better requirement — one the
customer is glad to have, and one the team is well positioned to answer. It
works by *educating*: showing the customer what is technically possible, what
good looks like, what risks to avoid, and what evaluation approach would surface
real quality. It does not work by sneaking the team's name into the
requirement.

Good shaping makes the customer smarter. If a shaping idea only helps because it
secretly disadvantages competitors or relies on private information, it is not
shaping — it is a risk.

## Shaping vehicles

The legitimate, public or semi-public channels for influencing a requirement:

- **Responses to sources-sought notices and RFIs.** Demonstrate capability and,
  where the notice invites it, comment on the approach. These also inform the
  government's set-aside decision.
- **White papers and capability briefings.** Offered to the customer to educate
  on technical possibilities and approaches. Substance over salesmanship.
- **Industry days and one-on-one sessions.** Attend, ask substantive questions,
  and present capability when the format allows.
- **Draft RFP comments.** The highest-value vehicle. A draft RFP explicitly
  invites industry to comment on scope, Section L, and Section M. Comment on
  what would make the evaluation fair, clear, and capable of distinguishing real
  quality.
- **Formal questions after RFP release.** Once the RFP is out, the Q&A process
  is the only shaping channel, and answers go to all bidders.

## Building the call plan

For each customer engagement the capture plan schedules, define:

- **Who** — the specific customer contact (named person or role).
- **Objective** — what this engagement is meant to achieve: learn a specific
  thing (close an intelligence gap), educate on a specific point, or build a
  specific relationship. One clear objective per engagement.
- **Vehicle** — which channel from the list above.
- **Owner** — who on the capture team will conduct it.
- **Timing** — when, relative to RFP release.
- **Key messages and key questions** — what the team will say and what it needs
  to learn. The questions map directly to the intelligence gaps in the capture
  plan.

Every engagement should both *give* (educate the customer) and *get* (close an
intelligence gap). An engagement with no learning objective wastes access.

## Timing relative to RFP release

- **Early capture** — broad relationship building and customer education.
  Influence the acquisition strategy itself.
- **Draft RFP window** — the peak shaping window. Concentrate effort on draft
  RFP comments and substantive engagement.
- **RFP release** — the customer-contact blackout begins. The call plan's
  active engagements should be substantially complete. After release, only the
  formal Q&A channel remains, and the plan tracks question submissions and
  amendment monitoring instead.
