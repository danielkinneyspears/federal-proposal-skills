# Capture Plan Structure

What a federal capture plan contains and what each section must do. The capture
plan integrates the other capture artifacts; it does not duplicate them.

## Contents
- Principles
- Section 1 — Opportunity summary
- Section 2 — Customer analysis
- Section 3 — Requirement analysis
- Section 4 — Competitive position
- Section 5 — Our position
- Section 6 — Win strategy summary
- Section 7 — Teaming
- Section 8 — Customer engagement plan
- Section 9 — Capture action list
- Section 10 — Capture schedule and gates
- Section 11 — Pursuit risks

## Principles

- **Living document.** The capture plan is updated continuously, not written
  once. Every update re-dates the artifact and notes what changed.
- **Mark confidence.** Every customer and competitive fact is tagged *known*
  (verified), *assessed* (inferred from evidence), or *unknown*. Unknowns are
  not failures; they are the work.
- **Integrate, do not duplicate.** Competitive analysis, price-to-win, and win
  strategy live in their own artifacts. The capture plan summarizes each in a
  few lines and links to the artifact.
- **Everything resolves to an action.** Any gap, risk, or unknown that matters
  appears in the action list with an owner and a date.

## Section 1 — Opportunity summary

A short factual header drawn from `00-opportunity-profile.md`: customer,
requirement, contract type and estimated value, period of performance,
set-aside, vehicle, anticipated RFP date, and the current bid/no-bid posture
from `01-bid-decision.md`.

## Section 2 — Customer analysis

The deepest section. Cover:

- **Mission and the problem.** What the customer organization exists to do, and
  the specific problem or pressure driving this acquisition.
- **Stakeholder and influence map.** The program office, the end users, and
  every named decision-maker and influencer — program manager, technical leads,
  contracting officer, requirements owner, and anyone shaping the acquisition.
  For each person record: role; what they care about; their **influence** over
  the award (high / medium / low); their **disposition** toward the team
  (advocate / neutral / skeptical / unknown); and the **quality of the team's
  access** to them (strong / limited / none). The map shows where relationship
  effort should go — a high-influence skeptic the team cannot reach is the most
  important name on the page, and the gap is a capture action.
- **Contact reports and engagement history.** A running record of customer
  engagements: who met whom, when, what was discussed, what the customer
  signaled, and what message the team delivered. Each engagement also records
  the **next legitimate touch** — the next planned contact, its objective, and
  its timing. Contact reports turn scattered interactions into intelligence and
  keep the engagement plan honest.
- **Hot buttons.** The issues the customer cares about most — past pain, risk
  fears, political pressures, mission priorities. These drive win themes later.
- **Biases and preferences.** Known leanings — toward incumbents, toward
  particular technical approaches, toward low risk, toward innovation.
- **Budget signals.** What is known or assessed about funding: the budget line,
  the independent government cost estimate if signaled, appropriation status.
- **Acquisition history.** How the customer bought this or similar work before,
  who won, how it went.

## Section 3 — Requirement analysis

- The scope as currently understood, and how it is expected to evolve through
  draft RFP and RFP.
- The likely evaluation emphasis — what the customer will probably reward in
  Section M, based on engagement and history.
- Open questions about scope that customer engagement should resolve.

## Section 4 — Competitive position

A summary of `03-competitive-assessment.md`: the likely bidders, the incumbent
and its standing, and where the team sits in the field. If that artifact does
not yet exist, this section states that the competitive assessment is a pending
capture action.

## Section 5 — Our position

An honest read of the team's standing for *this* customer and requirement:
strengths to build on, weaknesses to mitigate, relevant past performance, and
relationship assets. Weaknesses named here become mitigation actions.

## Section 6 — Win strategy summary

A summary of `05-win-strategy.md`: the discriminators, the win themes, and the
price posture. If that artifact does not yet exist, this section states that win
strategy development is a pending action and gives the current working
hypothesis.

## Section 7 — Teaming

- Capability and past-performance gaps that require partners.
- Candidate partners and their fit.
- The status of each teaming relationship and the actions to secure agreements
  before they constrain the bid.
- Any exclusivity, OCI, or small-business participation considerations.

## Section 8 — Customer engagement plan

The call plan. See `customer-engagement.md` for how to build it. Summarized
here: who will be engaged, the objective of each engagement, the shaping vehicle,
and the timing.

## Section 9 — Capture action list

The operational core. A table of actions, each with:

- the action, stated concretely;
- the **owner** (a named person or role);
- the **due date**;
- the **gate** it supports;
- status (open / in progress / done).

Every intelligence gap, every weakness mitigation, every engagement, and every
teaming need appears here. Sort by due date.

## Section 10 — Capture schedule and gates

The timeline from now to RFP release and proposal submission: draft RFP date,
RFP date, gate reviews, and the proposal milestones. Capture actions map to this
schedule.

## Section 11 — Pursuit risks

Risks to *winning the pursuit* (distinct from risks of *performing the work*):
an entrenched incumbent, a teaming partner not yet secured, unconfirmed funding,
a compressed schedule, a capability gap. For each: likelihood, impact, and the
mitigating action (which also appears in Section 9).
