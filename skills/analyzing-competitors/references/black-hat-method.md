# The Black Hat Method

How to profile competitors, bid the job as them, compare the field, and turn the
result into counters and ghosting opportunities.

## What a Black Hat is for

A Black Hat answers one question: *what will we actually be competing against?*
Its output is not a competitor directory — it is a set of predicted offers
concrete enough that the win strategy can be built to beat them. If the
assessment does not change what the proposal will say, it failed.

## Competitor profiling dimensions

For each credible bidder, build a profile across these dimensions. Tag every
item *known* (verified from a proper source) or *assessed* (inferred).

- **Qualifications and fit** — size status, set-aside eligibility, vehicle
  holdings, clearances. Can they even bid this?
- **Relevant past performance** — comparable contracts, scale, recency, and
  known performance quality (e.g., public CPARS-driven reputation, prior award
  history).
- **Customer relationship and incumbency** — are they the incumbent? Do they
  have relationships in this program office? How current?
- **Typical technical approach** — how this competitor usually solves this class
  of problem; their known methodologies, tools, and partnerships.
- **Typical price posture** — do they usually bid aggressively low, at market,
  or premium? What is their cost structure (large primes carry higher overhead;
  small firms often price lean)?
- **Capacity and commitments** — are they staffed and hungry for this work, or
  stretched across other pursuits and contracts?
- **Recent wins and losses** — relevant recent awards and losses, and what they
  signal about momentum and strategy.

## Bidding the job as a competitor

This is the core technique. For each competitor, drop the team's own
perspective entirely and answer as that competitor's capture lead:

1. **Would we bid this, and how badly do we want it?** Some competitors will
   no-bid. Assess their motivation honestly.
2. **What solution would we propose?** Given their typical approach,
   capabilities, and partners — what is their most likely technical solution?
3. **What would our win themes be?** What would this competitor claim as its
   discriminators against this customer?
4. **Where are we strong on this bid?** Their genuine advantages.
5. **Where are we vulnerable?** Their genuine weaknesses for *this* requirement
   and *this* customer.
6. **What price posture would we take?** Aggressive, market, or premium — and
   why, given their cost structure and how much they want the work.

Give each competitor their *best plausible bid*. The discipline is adversarial
empathy: if the prediction makes a competitor look foolish, it is wrong.

## The comparison matrix

Build a matrix scoring each competitor — and the team — across the factors the
customer will actually evaluate (drawn from Section M expectations, or the best
read of them pre-RFP). Use a simple scale (e.g., 1–5).

| Factor | Competitor A | Competitor B | Incumbent | Us |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Technical approach | | | | |
| Relevant past performance | | | | |
| Customer relationship | | | | |
| Key personnel / staffing | | | | |
| Management / execution risk | | | | |
| Likely price posture | | | | |

The matrix shows where the field clusters and where real separation exists.
Factors where everyone scores alike are not discriminators for anyone — the win
will be decided on the factors where scores spread.

## Most-probable-winner analysis

From the matrix and the predicted bids, state who is most likely to win *today*,
before the team's win strategy is applied — and why. Name the realistic cases
where the team is not the front-runner. This is the honest baseline the win
strategy has to move. Overstating the team's position here guarantees a weak
strategy.

## Deriving counters

For each competitor's predicted strengths and win themes, decide how the team's
proposal should respond:

- **Match** — neutralize a competitor strength by being clearly adequate on it,
  so it stops being a discriminator for them.
- **Beat** — out-perform them on a factor where the team genuinely can, and make
  the contrast visible to the evaluator.
- **Reframe** — shift the evaluator's attention toward factors where the team is
  strong and the competitor is not.

Each counter becomes an input to `developing-win-strategy`.

## Ghosting legitimately

Ghosting raises doubt about a competitor's likely approach without naming the
competitor. Done well, it is the evaluator recognizing a risk on their own
because the proposal emphasized the right contrast.

Legitimate ghosting:
- Targets a *real, evidence-based* weakness in a competitor's likely approach.
- Is framed as a positive: state the team's strength and *why it matters*, so
  the absence of that strength elsewhere becomes the evaluator's own inference.
- Names no competitor and makes no disparaging claim about a named firm.

Illegitimate ghosting — making unsupported negative claims, disparaging a named
competitor, or implying inside knowledge of a competitor's proposal — is both an
integrity problem and unpersuasive. Do not produce it.

Example of the legitimate form: instead of "Competitor X's offshore model
creates security risk," write "Our entirely US-based, cleared delivery team
eliminates the data-handling exposure that offshore models introduce" — true of
the team, and it lets the evaluator draw the contrast.

## Naming our own exposures

Run the Black Hat on the team itself: what will competitors ghost about the
team's likely bid? Every exposure identified here is a weakness the win strategy
and the proposal must actively mitigate — by fixing it, by pre-empting it, or by
reframing around it. An exposure that is known and unaddressed is the most
predictable way to lose.
