# Competitive Intelligence Ethics

What competitor information may and may not be gathered and used in a federal
competitive assessment. Read this before any competitor research. This skill
must stay inside these lines and must never recommend crossing them.

## The principle

Competitive intelligence in federal contracting is the disciplined analysis of
*public and legitimately available* information. It is forecasting, not
espionage. The line is simple: information that anyone could lawfully obtain is
fair to analyze; information that is non-public, proprietary, or
source-selection-sensitive is not — regardless of how it became available.

## In bounds — proper sources

- **Government award and contract data.** Publicly released award notices,
  contract histories, and spending data.
- **Publicly available past-performance reputation.** What is publicly known
  about how firms performed on prior work.
- **Public-facing competitor material.** Websites, press releases, published
  capability statements, marketing, conference presentations, job postings.
- **Public market research.** Industry analyses, trade press, published studies.
- **The team's own prior experience.** Past head-to-head competitions,
  the team's own debriefs, and direct observation of how a competitor operates —
  the team's own knowledge, not the competitor's protected information.
- **General industry knowledge.** Known cost-structure patterns (large primes
  carry higher overhead; lean firms price tighter), typical methodologies, and
  publicly known partnerships.

## Out of bounds — never use

- **Competitor proprietary information.** Cost data, technical solutions,
  internal strategy, pricing models, or proposal content that the competitor
  has not made public — however it was obtained.
- **Source-selection-sensitive information.** Anything non-public about the
  government's evaluation, the competitive range, other offerors' proposals or
  rankings, or the independent government cost estimate. Protected by the
  Procurement Integrity Act.
- **Information from a competitor's current or former employees** that is the
  competitor's protected proprietary information or trade secret.
- **Information obtained through misrepresentation** — pretexting, posing as a
  customer or partner, or any deception to extract competitor information.
- **Anything a confidentiality or non-disclosure obligation covers.**

## Handling questionable information

If competitor information of uncertain provenance surfaces during analysis:

- Do not use it until its source is known and confirmed to be proper.
- If it appears to be proprietary or source-selection-sensitive, the correct
  action is to quarantine it and escalate to legal or contracts — not to fold it
  quietly into the assessment.
- This skill should explicitly flag questionable information rather than
  incorporate it. When the user supplies a competitor "fact," and its source is
  unclear, ask where it came from before relying on it.

## How this constrains the assessment

- Competitor solutions, pricing, and themes in this assessment are **predictions
  built from public information and analysis** — always labeled *assessed*, never
  stated as known fact about what a competitor will actually do.
- An intelligence gap is closed by better public research or legitimate customer
  engagement, never by improper information. A gap that cannot be closed
  properly stays a gap.
- The assessment's value comes from rigorous analysis of legitimate
  information. That is both the ethical floor and, because improperly obtained
  information can disqualify a bid or trigger criminal liability, the only
  sound business practice.
