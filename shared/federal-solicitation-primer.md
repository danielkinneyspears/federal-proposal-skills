# Federal Solicitation Primer

How a US Federal solicitation is structured, how source selection works, and
which parts of the document govern the proposal. Skills reference this file
instead of re-explaining federal acquisition basics. It is a working primer for
proposal purposes, not a substitute for the FAR itself or for contracts/legal
review.

## Regulatory currency — read first

This primer describes durable structures of federal acquisition — the Uniform
Contract Format, the FAR Part 15 source-selection process, the tradeoff and
LPTA approaches. These change slowly. But specifics do change: the FAR is
amended, agencies maintain their own supplements (DFARS and others), dollar
thresholds are adjusted, and any individual solicitation can deviate from the
general pattern.

The skills in this package treat this primer as orienting background, not as
authority. For any task where a specific rule, threshold, or deadline is
load-bearing — debrief request windows, late-proposal rules, size standards,
protest timelines, certification requirements — verify the **current** FAR
section, the applicable **agency supplement**, and above all the **actual
solicitation text** before relying on it. When a skill is doing
deadline-sensitive or legally adjacent work, it says so and recommends
confirming the current rules and, where the stakes warrant, involving contracts
or legal counsel.

The governing order is always: the specific solicitation first, then the
applicable regulation, then this primer last.

## The Uniform Contract Format (Sections A–M)

Most negotiated solicitations use the Uniform Contract Format (UCF), defined at
FAR 15.204-1. It has four parts and Sections A through M.

**Part I — The Schedule**
- **A — Solicitation/contract form.** Cover form (SF 33, or SF 1449 for commercial).
- **B — Supplies or services and prices/costs.** The contract line items (CLINs). The cost/price volume prices these.
- **C — Description / specifications / statement of work.** The requirement. Often a SOW, PWS, or SOO delivered as a Section J attachment.
- **D — Packaging and marking.**
- **E — Inspection and acceptance.**
- **F — Deliveries or performance.** Period of performance, place of performance, delivery schedule.
- **G — Contract administration data.** Invoicing, accounting, points of contact.
- **H — Special contract requirements.** Often important: key personnel clauses, OCI requirements, security, option provisions.

**Part II — Contract Clauses**
- **I — Contract clauses.** FAR/DFARS/agency clauses incorporated by reference or full text.

**Part III — List of Documents, Exhibits, and Other Attachments**
- **J — List of attachments.** Where the SOW/PWS, CDRLs, QASP, wage determinations, and government-furnished property lists usually live.

**Part IV — Representations and Instructions**
- **K — Representations, certifications, and other statements of offerors.** Things the offeror must certify.
- **L — Instructions, conditions, and notices to offerors.** *How to prepare and submit the proposal.*
- **M — Evaluation factors for award.** *How the government will evaluate and decide.*

Not every solicitation uses the full UCF. Commercial-item and task-order
solicitations often compress or rename sections, but the *functions* of L, M,
and C are always present somewhere. Find them by function, not section letter.

## The proposal triad: read M, then L, then C

Three sections govern the proposal. Their relationship is the single most
important thing to get right.

- **Section M — evaluation factors.** What the government rewards. The order and
  weighting of factors here is the customer telling you where to spend effort.
- **Section L — instructions.** How to organize, format, and submit. Volume
  structure, page limits, fonts, file formats, due dates.
- **Section C / SOW / PWS / SOO — the requirement.** The actual work.

**Read M first.** It defines what "winning" means. Then read L to learn how the
proposal must be packaged to be scored against M. Then read C to learn the work
you are committing to. A proposal organized around L but blind to M is compliant
and unpersuasive. A proposal that addresses C but ignores M answers a question
the evaluator was not asked to score.

When L and M disagree (a factor in M with no corresponding instruction in L, or
vice versa), treat it as a real ambiguity: submit a question to the contracting
officer. When this package's general guidance conflicts with a specific
solicitation's Section L, **Section L wins.**

## How FAR Part 15 source selection works

Negotiated procurements follow FAR Part 15, Subpart 15.3. The arc:

1. **Solicitation released.** Often preceded by a sources-sought notice, RFI, or draft RFP.
2. **Proposals received** by the due date and time. Late is late; FAR 15.208 governs late proposals strictly.
3. **Evaluation** (FAR 15.305). The Source Selection Evaluation Board rates each proposal against the Section M factors. Past performance is evaluated for recency, relevancy, and quality. Cost/price is always evaluated.
4. **Competitive range** (FAR 15.306), if discussions will be held. The contracting officer establishes the range of the most highly rated proposals.
5. **Discussions.** The government issues Evaluation Notices identifying weaknesses, deficiencies, and issues. Offerors in the range respond. Discussions may materially change a proposal; clarifications and communications may not.
6. **Final Proposal Revisions (FPRs).** Offerors submit their last revisions.
7. **Source selection decision** (FAR 15.308). The Source Selection Authority makes the integrated best-value (or LPTA) decision and documents the rationale.
8. **Award and notification.**
9. **Debriefs** (FAR 15.506). Unsuccessful (and successful) offerors may request a debrief explaining the evaluation of their proposal and the basis for award.

Not every procurement has discussions; an agency may award on initial proposals
if the solicitation says so. Assume your initial proposal could be your only
proposal.

## Evaluation approaches: tradeoff vs LPTA

Section M states the evaluation approach. The two principal ones:

- **Best-value tradeoff** (FAR 15.101-1). The government may pay a price premium
  for superior non-price merit. Strengths matter: exceeding a requirement in a
  way that benefits the government can justify a higher price. Write to *win*
  the tradeoff — show value, not just compliance.
- **LPTA — Lowest-Price Technically Acceptable** (FAR 15.101-2). Non-price
  factors are pass/fail. Among technically acceptable proposals, lowest price
  wins. Exceeding a requirement earns nothing and can raise price. Write to be
  *cleanly acceptable* at the lowest defensible price; do not gold-plate.

Many evaluations are hybrids (some factors LPTA-style acceptability, some
tradeoff). The skills adapt drafting and review posture to the stated approach,
so identifying it early matters.

## Task orders under IDIQ (FAR 16.505)

A large share of federal work is competed as task or delivery orders under
multiple-award IDIQ contracts and GWACs, governed by the **fair opportunity**
procedures of FAR 16.505 — not the full Part 15 process. Practical differences:

- The "solicitation" is often a Task Order Request (TOR) or RFQ, frequently
  shorter, with compressed L/M-equivalent instructions.
- Turnaround is faster; page limits are tighter; oral presentations and
  demonstrations are common.
- Only IDIQ holders may compete. Teaming is constrained by who holds the vehicle.
- Debrief rights exist but are governed by 16.505 and thresholds, not 15.506.

The lifecycle and skills apply, but with compressed timelines and a stricter
premium on concise, directly responsive writing.

## Commercial and simplified acquisitions

- **Commercial products and services** (FAR Part 12) use SF 1449 and may be
  issued as a combined synopsis/solicitation. Instructions and evaluation are
  often abbreviated.
- **Simplified acquisitions** (FAR Part 13) below the simplified acquisition
  threshold use streamlined procedures, often an RFQ.

In all cases, the proposal triad still applies: find the instructions, find the
evaluation basis, find the requirement.

## Reading order for a new solicitation

1. **The notice and due date.** When is it due, how is it submitted, what is the contract type and set-aside status.
2. **Section M.** What is evaluated, in what order, under what approach.
3. **Section L.** Volume structure, page limits, format rules, submission mechanics.
4. **Section C / SOW / PWS / SOO** and its Section J attachments.
5. **Section H.** Special requirements — key personnel, OCI, security, options.
6. **Section B.** CLIN structure, for the cost volume.
7. **Section K and the clauses.** Representations and certifications; flow-down obligations.
8. **Amendments and Q&A.** Re-read M and L after every amendment.
