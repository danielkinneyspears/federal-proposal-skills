# Glossary — Federal Capture and Proposal Terms

## Solicitation and acquisition documents

- **Solicitation** — The government's request for offers. Umbrella term covering RFP, RFQ, and RFI.
- **RFP (Request for Proposal)** — Solicitation for a negotiated procurement, normally under FAR Part 15. Asks for proposals that will be evaluated and may involve discussions.
- **RFQ (Request for Quotation)** — Used for simplified acquisitions and many task/delivery orders. Vendors submit quotes; a quote is not an offer the government can simply accept.
- **RFI (Request for Information)** — Market research instrument. No award results. Used to shape an eventual acquisition.
- **Sources Sought Notice** — Market research notice asking whether capable sources exist, often to inform a small-business set-aside decision. Responding well is a capture activity.
- **Draft RFP (DRFP)** — A pre-release draft issued for industry comment. The single best capture-phase opportunity to influence the requirement and Section M.
- **Amendment** — A formal change to the solicitation. Amendments can change requirements, Section L, Section M, or the due date. Each must be acknowledged.
- **Q&A / Questions and Answers** — Government responses to offeror questions, issued as an amendment or attachment. Q&A often clarifies evaluation intent.

## Solicitation structure (Uniform Contract Format)

The Uniform Contract Format (UCF) organizes most FAR Part 15 solicitations into Sections A–M. See `federal-solicitation-primer.md` for detail.

- **Section C** — Description / specifications / statement of work. The *requirement*.
- **Section L** — Instructions, conditions, and notices to offerors. *How to prepare and submit the proposal.*
- **Section M** — Evaluation factors for award. *How the government will score the proposal and decide.*
- **SOW (Statement of Work)** — Prescriptive description of the work to be performed.
- **PWS (Performance Work Statement)** — Performance-based work statement: states outcomes and standards, not methods. Paired with a QASP.
- **SOO (Statement of Objectives)** — States the government's objectives and lets the offeror propose the SOW/PWS. Maximum solution freedom.
- **CDRL (Contract Data Requirements List)** — The list of data deliverables (reports, plans) the contract requires; individual items are DD Form 1423.
- **CLIN (Contract Line Item Number)** — A priced line item in Section B. The cost volume prices CLINs.
- **QASP (Quality Assurance Surveillance Plan)** — How the government will measure performance against a PWS.

## Contract types and vehicles

- **FFP (Firm-Fixed-Price)** — Contractor bears cost risk; price does not change with cost.
- **T&M (Time-and-Materials)** — Paid at fixed labor rates plus materials at cost.
- **Cost-reimbursement (CPFF / CPAF / CPIF)** — Government reimburses allowable cost plus a fixed, award, or incentive fee.
- **IDIQ (Indefinite-Delivery/Indefinite-Quantity)** — A "hunting license": a base contract under which the government later issues task or delivery orders.
- **Task Order (TO) / Delivery Order (DO)** — An order placed under an IDIQ or other ordering vehicle.
- **Fair Opportunity** — FAR 16.505 requirement that each awardee under a multiple-award IDIQ get a fair chance to be considered for each order above the micro-purchase threshold.
- **GWAC (Government-Wide Acquisition Contract)** — A government-wide IDIQ for IT.
- **MAS / GSA Schedule** — GSA's Multiple Award Schedule: pre-negotiated commercial contracts agencies order against.
- **BPA (Blanket Purchase Agreement)** — A simplified ordering arrangement, often established against a Schedule.
- **Set-aside** — An acquisition reserved for a category of business (small business, 8(a), SDVOSB, WOSB, HUBZone).

## Source selection and evaluation

- **Best-value tradeoff** — Source selection that may pay a price premium for higher non-price merit (FAR 15.101-1).
- **LPTA (Lowest-Price Technically Acceptable)** — Award to the lowest price that meets a pass/fail acceptability standard (FAR 15.101-2). No credit for exceeding requirements.
- **Evaluation factor / subfactor** — A defined dimension of merit in Section M (e.g., Technical Approach, Management Approach, Past Performance, Price).
- **Strength** — A proposal aspect that exceeds a requirement in a way that benefits the government.
- **Weakness** — A flaw that increases the risk of unsuccessful performance.
- **Significant weakness** — A flaw that appreciably increases performance risk.
- **Deficiency** — A material failure to meet a requirement, or a combination of weaknesses that increases risk to an unacceptable level. A deficiency makes a proposal unawardable as written.
- **Adjectival rating** — The scoring scale (e.g., Outstanding / Good / Acceptable / Marginal / Unacceptable) applied to non-price factors.
- **Performance confidence** — The past-performance assessment: the government's confidence that the offeror will successfully perform (e.g., Substantial / Satisfactory / Limited / Unknown confidence).
- **Competitive range** — The set of most highly rated proposals with which the government may hold discussions (FAR 15.306).
- **Clarifications / Communications / Discussions** — Escalating levels of government–offeror exchange (FAR 15.306). Discussions can change a proposal; clarifications cannot.
- **Evaluation Notice (EN)** — A government-issued item during discussions identifying a weakness, deficiency, or issue for the offeror to address.
- **FPR (Final Proposal Revision)** — The offeror's last proposal submission after discussions close.
- **SSA / SSEB / SSAC** — Source Selection Authority (decides), Source Selection Evaluation Board (evaluates), Source Selection Advisory Council (advises). Roles defined in agency source selection procedures.
- **Debrief** — The government's post-award explanation to offerors of the evaluation of their proposal and the basis for award (FAR 15.506).
- **Protest** — A challenge to the award, filed with the agency, GAO, or the Court of Federal Claims.

## Capture and business development

- **Capture management** — The disciplined pre-RFP effort to position to win a specific opportunity.
- **Capture plan** — The living document recording customer knowledge, the requirement, competitive position, win strategy, teaming, and action items.
- **Bid/no-bid decision** — The gated decision to pursue (or decline) an opportunity, based on probability of win and value.
- **Gate review** — A milestone decision point in the BD lifecycle where leadership commits (or declines) resources to advance a pursuit.
- **pWin (probability of win)** — An estimate, ideally evidence-based, of the likelihood of winning a specific opportunity.
- **Hot button** — An issue the customer cares about intensely; the customer's evaluation will be shaped by it.
- **Win strategy** — The plan for how this pursuit will be won: which discriminators, which themes, which price posture.
- **Win theme** — A repeated, substantiated message that connects an offeror strength to a customer hot button and to a Section M factor.
- **Discriminator** — A capability or feature that is both valuable to the customer and *not* readily offered by competitors. The basis of a real win theme.
- **Ghosting** — Raising doubt about a competitor's approach without naming the competitor, by emphasizing a contrast that favors your solution.
- **Black Hat** — A structured competitive simulation: the team plays competitors and models their likely offers and themes.
- **Price-to-Win (PTW)** — Analysis estimating the price (and price posture) needed to win, independent of a bottom-up cost estimate.
- **Teaming agreement** — An agreement between a prime and subcontractor(s) to pursue an opportunity together.
- **OCI (Organizational Conflict of Interest)** — A situation where other work, relationships, or access could bias performance or give an unfair advantage; can disqualify an offeror.
- **B&P (Bid and Proposal cost)** — The cost of pursuing and preparing proposals, an indirect cost the bid/no-bid decision spends.

## Proposal development and review

- **Proposal manager** — The person who owns the proposal schedule, compliance, and process; distinct from the capture manager who owns positioning.
- **Volume** — A major division of the proposal (e.g., Technical Volume, Management Volume, Past Performance Volume, Cost/Price Volume), defined by Section L.
- **Compliance matrix** — A table that cross-walks every solicitation requirement (Sections L, M, C) to the proposal location that addresses it. The spine of a compliant proposal.
- **Annotated outline** — A proposal outline built directly from Section L, with each section annotated with the requirements, evaluation factors, themes, and content it must contain.
- **Storyboard** — A pre-writing planning sheet for one proposal section: theme statement, customer issue, proof points, graphic concept, and compliance tags.
- **Mockup** — A page-level visual layout of a section before narrative is written.
- **Key personnel** — Individuals named in the proposal whose qualifications are evaluated and who are contractually committed to the effort.
- **Past performance** — Evidence of how well the offeror performed comparable prior work; evaluated for recency, relevancy, and quality.
- **CPARS (Contractor Performance Assessment Reporting System)** — The government system of record for past performance ratings.
- **PPQ (Past Performance Questionnaire)** — A reference form a customer completes about an offeror's prior performance.
- **Color team review** — A staged proposal review named by color. Pink (early-draft strategy and structure), Red (near-final, evaluator perspective), Gold (executive pre-submission). See `proposal-lifecycle.md`.
- **Pink Team** — Early review (~30–50% draft) of strategy, compliance approach, themes, and structure.
- **Red Team** — Near-final review (~90% draft) that scores the proposal as a source-selection evaluator would.
- **Gold Team** — Executive review just before submission; final go/no-go.
- **Recovery** — The work of fixing a proposal between a color team review and the next milestone.

## Pricing and cost

- **IGCE (Independent Government Cost Estimate)** — The government's own estimate of what the work should cost.
- **BOE (Basis of Estimate)** — The narrative and rationale supporting each element of the proposed cost: why these hours, this labor mix, these materials.
- **Cost realism** — In cost-reimbursement evaluations, the government's assessment of whether proposed costs are realistic for the work (FAR 15.404-1(d)). Unrealistically low cost can be adjusted upward for evaluation.
- **Price reasonableness** — Whether the proposed price is fair and not too high.
- **Cost-technical tradeoff** — The source selection judgment weighing price against non-price merit.
- **Wrap rate** — A fully burdened labor rate (direct labor plus indirect costs and fee).
- **Escalation** — The year-over-year increase applied to rates across a multi-year period of performance.
